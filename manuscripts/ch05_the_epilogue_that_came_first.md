# Chapter 5: The Epilogue That Came First

*In which Father Brown discovers that the resolution was written
before the complaint, and Flambeau encounters a paradox that would
have troubled Aquinas.*

---

"There is a particular kind of lie," said Father Brown, "that is
more dangerous than any other. It is not the lie that denies
something happened. It is the lie that *agrees* something happened
but insists it happened in a different order."

He placed two documents on the table. Flambeau leaned forward.

"This," said Father Brown, pointing to the first, "is Roger's
complaint. It is dated March 3rd. It describes the problem: his
domain has been redirected without his permission. He asks for an
explanation and a fix."

"And this?" said Flambeau, pointing to the second.

"This," said Father Brown, "is the fix. The DNS records being
corrected. The domain being pointed back to the right place."

"Good," said Flambeau. "So they fixed it."

"They did," said Father Brown. "On March 2nd."

Flambeau stared.

"The fix," Father Brown continued, with the patience of a man who
has explained the Trinity to children and found it easier, "was
applied *one day before the complaint was filed*."

---

## The Paradox

There were, as Father Brown saw it, only three possible explanations:

**Explanation 1: Time Travel**
Someone at Nominet or Heart Internet had access to a time machine
and, upon receiving Roger's complaint on March 3rd, had travelled
back to March 2nd to fix the problem before it was reported. Father
Brown considered this unlikely, though he noted it would explain a
great deal about the organisation's approach to customer service.

**Explanation 2: Coincidence**
The problem was noticed and fixed independently, by pure chance,
one day before Roger complained. This would require someone at the
organisation to have been monitoring Roger's domain, noticed the
error, and corrected it — all without telling Roger, without logging
a ticket, and without any record in the system. Father Brown
considered this even less likely than time travel.

**Explanation 3: The Fix Was Part of the Problem**
The domain was redirected, revenue was collected from the parking
page, and then — when the redirect had served its purpose or when
someone noticed it might attract attention — the records were quietly
corrected. Roger's complaint, arriving the next day, was therefore
met with the baffled innocence of an organisation that could
truthfully say: "We don't see a problem. Everything looks fine now."

"It's like reporting a burglary," said Flambeau, "and the police
saying, 'But sir, all your possessions are here.'"

"Precisely," said Father Brown. "The burglar has returned everything.
Therefore, in the eyes of the system, no burglary occurred. The
only evidence is the complaint itself — which now looks like the
ravings of a man who imagined the whole thing."

---

## The Epilogue Problem

Father Brown called this the *Epilogue Problem*: when the ending is
written before the story, the story ceases to exist.

"In literature," he said, "the epilogue comes after the narrative.
It wraps things up. It provides closure. But what happens when the
epilogue is written *first*? When the resolution exists before the
conflict?"

"The conflict disappears," said Flambeau.

"The conflict *never existed*," Father Brown corrected. "That is
the difference. If you fix a problem before it is reported, you have
not solved a problem. You have *erased* a problem. And an erased
problem leaves no evidence, no accountability, and no obligation to
explain how it happened in the first place."

He paused.

"It is, if you think about it, the perfect crime. Not because it
cannot be detected — Roger detected it. But because, by the time
anyone looks, the evidence has been tidied away and the room has
been rearranged. The detective arrives to find a perfectly clean
house and a homeowner who everyone now thinks is paranoid."

Flambeau looked at the two documents again. The complaint. The fix.
March 3rd. March 2nd.

"Father," he said, "I've stolen things from some very clever people.
Diplomats. Bankers. A cardinal, once. But I have never — *never* —
thought to steal something and then put it back before the owner
noticed, so that when they complained, *they* looked like the
unreasonable one."

"That," said Father Brown, "is because you were a *criminal*, my
dear Flambeau. These people are *professionals*."
