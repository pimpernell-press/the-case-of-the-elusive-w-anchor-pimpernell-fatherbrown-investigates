# Chapter 10: The Paradox of the Helpful Vandal

*Being an afterword, in which Father Brown considers the nature of
institutional harm, and arrives at a conclusion that is either very
simple or very complicated, depending on whether you work for
Nominet.*

---

Some weeks after the case of the elusive W-Anchor had been, in the
official terminology, "resolved" — which meant, in practice, that
everyone had stopped replying to Roger's emails — Father Brown found
himself in an unusual position. He was writing an essay.

Father Brown did not, as a rule, write essays. He wrote sermons,
which are a different thing entirely. A sermon aims to save souls.
An essay aims to pin them to a board and label them, which Father
Brown considered a less charitable enterprise. But this case had
lodged in his mind like a pebble in a shoe — not painful enough to
stop walking, but impossible to ignore.

The essay was titled: *The Paradox of the Helpful Vandal.*

---

## The Paradox, Stated

"A vandal," Father Brown wrote, in handwriting that his bishop had
once described as "evidence of either great holiness or a
neurological condition,"

> "A vandal who breaks your window is a simple problem. You call
> the police. You replace the window. You move on. But a vandal
> who breaks your window and then *installs a better one* — without
> your permission, at a time of their choosing, in a style you did
> not select — presents a far more complex problem.
>
> "Because when you complain, they say: 'But the new window is
> better.' And when you say, 'I didn't ask for a new window,' they
> say: 'You should be grateful.' And when you say, 'You broke the
> old one first,' they say: 'We have no record of that.'
>
> "This is the Paradox of the Helpful Vandal. The harm is real, but
> the evidence has been replaced by the remedy. The crime scene has
> been cleaned by the criminal. And the victim is left arguing not
> about what happened, but about whether they should be *thankful*
> for what happened."

---

## The Application

Nominet, Father Brown reflected, was the Helpful Vandal par
excellence.

Roger's domain was redirected. This was the broken window. Then
the domain was restored — before Roger had finished complaining.
This was the new window. And when Roger asked, "Who broke the old
window, and why?" he was told, in effect: "The window is fine now.
What are you complaining about?"

The paradox operated on three levels:

**Level 1: The Evidence Vanishes**
By fixing the problem before acknowledging it, the organisation
ensures that any investigator — ombudsman, regulator, journalist —
who examines the case finds a working domain and a closed ticket.
The *current state* is used to disprove the *historical state*.
It is as if a murderer could escape justice by resurrecting the
victim.

**Level 2: The Victim Becomes the Problem**
A person complaining about a problem that no longer exists looks,
to an outside observer, unreasonable. Obsessive. Paranoid. The
organisation does not need to discredit the complainant; the
timeline does it for them. "He's still going on about something
that was fixed weeks ago" is a sentence that has closed more
legitimate complaints than any corporate lawyer.

**Level 3: The System Learns**
Each successful application of the paradox teaches the organisation
that this method *works*. Fix fast, deny slow, wait for the
complainant to exhaust themselves. The process is not a failure —
it is an *optimisation*. It is, in the language of corporate
strategy, a *best practice*. And best practices, once established,
are very nearly impossible to dislodge, because the people who
benefit from them are the same people who would need to approve
their removal.

---

## The Moral

Father Brown put down his pen. He looked out of the vestry window
at the churchyard, where the graves of several centuries' worth of
parishioners lay in patient rows, each one a record of someone who
had existed and whose existence could not be retroactively denied,
however inconvenient it might be to the diocese.

"The moral," he wrote,

> "is not that institutions are evil. Most institutions are staffed
> by decent people who would be horrified to learn what their
> organisation does in aggregate. The moral is that *systems* are
> capable of producing outcomes that no individual within them
> intended, approved, or even noticed.
>
> "A hundred people, each doing their job correctly, can produce a
> result that is monstrous. This is not a paradox. It is the
> ordinary operation of bureaucracy. And the only defence against
> it is the thing that bureaucracies fear most: a complete, written,
> public, uneditable record of what happened, when it happened, and
> to whom.
>
> "Roger made such a record. You are reading it now."

---

*Father Brown closed his notebook, finished his tea (cold, as
usual), and went to hear confessions. None of the penitents that
afternoon had stolen a domain name, but Father Brown listened to
them with the same attention he gave to everything — the attention
of a man who knows that the smallest sins and the largest sins
operate by exactly the same mechanism, and that mechanism is the
quiet, persistent belief that what you are doing doesn't really
count.*
