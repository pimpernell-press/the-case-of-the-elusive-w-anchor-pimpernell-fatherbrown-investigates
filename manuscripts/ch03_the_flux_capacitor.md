# Chapter 3: The Flux Capacitor and the Sin of Helpfulness

*In which Father Brown traces a redirect through three layers of
misdirection, and encounters a frog with good intentions.*

---

## Part I: The Flux Capacitor

Father Brown had once heard a parishioner's child explain the plot of a
film in which a scientist built a time machine out of a sports car. The
key component was called a "flux capacitor," and its function, as far as
Father Brown could determine, was to make the impossible seem mechanical.

"That," said Father Brown, tapping the laptop screen with a finger that
had administered last rites more often than it had operated a trackpad,
"is what we have here. A flux capacitor. A mechanism designed to make
something impossible look like it happened by accident."

What the screen showed was a chain of redirects. When someone typed
`w-anchor.co.uk` into their browser, the following occurred:

1. The browser asked Nominet's registry: "Where is w-anchor.co.uk?"
2. Nominet's registry replied: "Ask these nameservers" — pointing to
   GoDaddy's infrastructure
3. GoDaddy's nameservers replied: "The website is over here" — pointing
   to a parking page
4. The parking page displayed: "This domain may be for sale" — with
   advertisements generating revenue for GoDaddy

"Four steps," said Father Brown. "Each one, taken individually, looks
like a technical process. A machine doing machine things. But taken
together, they describe something quite different."

"Which is?" said Flambeau.

"A pickpocket," said Father Brown. "Step one: bump into the victim.
Step two: apologise profusely. Step three: walk away. Step four: spend
the money. The genius is that each step, observed in isolation, looks
like an accident or a courtesy. It is only when you watch the whole
sequence that you see the hand going into the pocket."

---

## Part II: The Frog and the Scorpion

There is an old fable about a scorpion who asks a frog to carry him
across a river. The frog says, "But you'll sting me." The scorpion
says, "Why would I? If I sting you, we both drown." Halfway across,
the scorpion stings the frog. As they sink, the frog asks, "Why?" The
scorpion replies, "It's my nature."

Father Brown had always found this fable unsatisfying.

"The real version," he told Flambeau, "is rather different. The
scorpion asks the frog to carry him across. The frog refuses. So the
scorpion sets up a *ferry service*. He hires other frogs to carry
passengers. He obtains a licence. He publishes a timetable. He wins
an award for *services to river-crossing*. And then, very quietly, he
begins stinging the passengers — but only the ones who don't check
their tickets carefully."

"And when someone complains?"

"The scorpion expresses *concern*," said Father Brown. "He launches
an *internal review*. He appoints a *complaints officer* — who is,
of course, another scorpion. And he sends the complaining frog a
letter explaining that the sting was actually a *service enhancement*
and that the frog should have read the terms and conditions more
carefully."

This, Father Brown explained, was the Nominet model.

When Roger's domain was redirected, it was not described as a
*seizure* or a *theft* or even an *error*. It was described as a
*service*. The parking page was presented as a *convenience* — a
helpful placeholder that existed *for the domain owner's benefit*.

"The sin of helpfulness," said Father Brown, "is the most dangerous
sin of all. A thief who admits he is stealing can be caught. A thief
who insists he is *helping* can only be caught by someone willing to
argue about the definition of help. And that, I assure you, is an
argument that can last longer than the theft."
