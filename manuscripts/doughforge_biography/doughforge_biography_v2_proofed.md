# The DoughForge Biography of Roger G. Lewis

*A profile in eight movements, from the colliery scales to the housing platform*

---

## I. The Valley

Foster Lewis was born in 1902 in Tonyrefail, in the Rhondda Valley, and he went underground before he was old enough to vote. He was a check-weighman at Glyn Colliery — the man elected by his fellow miners to stand at the company's scales and verify the weight of coal against the colliery's own measurement. The colliery had one set of scales. The check-weighman brought another. The discrepancy between the two was never an accident.

He was also a Welsh Baptist deacon and a hairdresser in the evenings — a man who served the community's soul on Sundays and its vanity on weeknights, and saw no contradiction between the two. He read Burns, Tennyson, Omar Khayyam, and the King James Bible. He wrote a memoir in his eighties, dictating to his wife Doris because his eyesight was failing, which he called *The Miner's Tale*. It was typed by his granddaughter Suzanne and posted back and forth between Oxfordshire and Talbot Green until it was printed for the family in 1985. In the preface, he asked his readers "to accept my efforts for their substance untouched by any academic."

Deuteronomy 25:13–16: *Do not have two differing weights in your bag — one heavy, one light.* Foster Lewis lived inside that verse every working day. The verse was not theology for a man paid by the ton. It was the ground state — the moral minimum below which the arithmetic of a life becomes fraud.

He noted, in one of the memoir's quieter passages, that "Welsh as between Welsh speaking married couples was the language of Endearment." Not the language of politics or commerce or complaint. The language of endearment. The soft register, reserved for the people you love, spoken at the kitchen table after the public language of the colliery and the chapel and the union hall had been put away for the night.

His grandson Roger would inherit that distinction between public and private registers. He would spend forty years acquiring public languages — surveying, property, finance, economics, poetry, blogging, platform architecture — before discovering that the private language, the kitchen-table language, was the one that carried the weight.

## II. The Formation

Roger Glyndwr Lewis grew up reading. Not casually — compulsively, omnivorously, and without a syllabus. Blake before breakfast. Shelley at school. Coleridge on the bus. Ruskin discovered in a library and never put down. C.S. Peirce encountered in a footnote and pursued across three decades of increasingly technical philosophy. G.K. Chesterton read for the paradoxes and kept for the theology. Will Cuppy read for the jokes and kept for the method — the deadpan footnote that demolishes the main text is Cuppy's signature, and it became one of Lewis's.

He began writing poetry as a teenager and has not stopped. The poems accumulated across decades — usury odes, housing elegies, BIS haiku cycles, Peircean sonnets, a philippic against the Circle of Blame that runs to fourteen orations in the Ciceronian mode. He filed them under "Poetry" in his TheBrain knowledge map, a node with eighty-six children and three lateral jump links connecting the Bank for International Settlements to the Going Direct Paradigm to a retrospective on his own decade-old instrument. The poetry was never a sideline. It was the integrating layer — the I-quadrant, in Ken Wilber's AQAL framework, that connected the economics to the ethics to the experience.

In 2011, the poems found a public outlet. Lewis began blogging under the title *Not The Grub Street Journal* — later renamed *Grub Street in Exile* when the operation moved to Substack. The blog ran for fifteen years, producing hundreds of posts that mixed monetary theory with literary criticism with personal testimony with invective. He found his people: David Malone, whose *Golem XIV* blog provided the analytical framework; John Ward at *The Slog*, who wrote from exile with the same fury; and a network of independent thinkers who called themselves, with appropriate self-deprecation, the Three Pamphleteers. Grub Street was not a metaphor. It was a postal address — the street where writers lived who could not afford to live anywhere else, writing what nobody would pay them to write, because someone had to.

## III. The Charter

On 14 December 1990, Roger Lewis was elected a Professional Associate of the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors — the Valuation branch. He had trained at Shell UK, Prudential Assurance, London Shop PLC, and Hillier Parker May & Rowden. His final-year thesis concerned the Contractors Principle of Valuation for the taxation of gas terminals in Scotland, written during a placement with one of the Seven Sisters oil companies. He has been self-employed since the date of his election. The charter is now thirty-five years old.

A chartered surveyor is, at root, a person who measures things and tells you what they are worth. The instruments vary — the theodolite for angles, the chain for distances, the Red Book for valuations — but the discipline is the same: stand at a known point, sight through the instrument, read the scale, report the measurement. Do not confuse the measurement with the thing measured. Do not confuse the valuation with the value.

Lewis describes trigonometry as "pound-shop geometry" — the mathematics of triangles applied to land, which is to say the mathematics of relationships between fixed points applied to the thing that everybody fights over. The theodolite is a diagnostic instrument. It tells you where you are in relation to where you have been and where you are looking. It does not tell you where to go. That requires a decision the instrument cannot make.

He would spend the next thirty-five years learning the difference between a measurement and a decision — and building instruments that made the difference visible.

## IV. The Docklands / Property Years

Lewis entered professional practice in 1990 and spent the following decade in the regeneration of London Docklands. The RLD Group, which he founded and led, completed hundreds of transactions totalling over three million square feet of property. The offices were on the Isle of Dogs. The team was small. The work was large.

The flagship concept was East 14.com — "The Networked Village," a mixed-use waterside development opposite the Millennium Dome. Live-work conversions, datacentres, residential towers, a community structured around digital infrastructure that did not yet have a vocabulary. It was, twenty years before the term existed, a platform — a Dashboard for living and working in the same place. The concept was ahead of the technology. The technology caught up. The development did not survive the journey.

During these years, Lewis also wrote. Not publicly — the Scriblerus writing, he calls it, after the club of Pope and Swift and Gay, who wrote satire under a collective pseudonym while maintaining their public careers. The property developer wrote poetry about usury. The surveyor wrote essays about monetary reform. The man who valued three million square feet of Docklands for profit was privately assembling the intellectual architecture that would, two decades later, become the FAIR-Index.

The property years made Lewis financially independent. They also made him, by his own account, "very selfish and ultimately very ill." The professional career was successful by any conventional standard. The human cost was not entered on any balance sheet. The check-weighman's grandson had learned to read the company's scales fluently. He had not yet learned to bring his own.

## V. The Conquest of Dough

*The Conquest of Dough* is a novel Lewis wrote between 2016 and 2024. It is a satirical epic about bread, money, and the relationship between the two. The title is a pun — "dough" meaning both the substance you bake and the substance you bank — and the pun is the argument.

The novel's origin is a story about Syrian sourdough from Aleppo. A baker, displaced by war, carries his starter culture across borders because the yeast is alive and irreplaceable — a living organism that connects the bread of today to the bread of last week and the bread of last year and the bread of the city that was destroyed. The starter is the anchor. Without it, you can still make bread, but it will not be *this* bread. It will not carry the memory.

Lewis describes the work as "a trilogy in four parts" — the structure borrowed from Douglas Adams, the content borrowed from the history of money. The novel was published through Draft2Digital alongside ten other books that Lewis produced between 2018 and 2026, all self-published, all built using the reproducible publishing toolkit he developed in parallel with the writing.

David Malone — filmmaker, author of *The Debt Generation*, and one of Lewis's closest intellectual collaborators — provided the endorsement that sits at the front of the work: "Here is a poet who can engage with the pressing and vital concerns of our times. As did Blake in his time." The comparison is neither casual nor inflated. Blake engraved his own books, invented his own mythology, and spent his life insisting that the mental fight was the real fight. Lewis builds his own publishing pipeline, maintains his own version control, and has spent fifteen years insisting that the monetary fight is the moral fight. The methods rhyme across two centuries.

## VI. The Monetary Argument

The intellectual core of Lewis's work — the argument that runs beneath the poetry, the novel, the blog, and the housing platform — is a monetary argument. It can be stated in three propositions.

First: banks create money at the point of lending. This is not a conspiracy theory. It is the position of the Bank of England, stated explicitly in their 2014 Quarterly Bulletin: "Whenever a bank makes a loan, it simultaneously creates a matching deposit in the borrower's bank account, thereby creating new money." Richard Werner demonstrated the mechanism empirically. The process is called credit creation. Most money in circulation was created this way.

Second: money is a ratio. Alexander Del Mar, the nineteenth-century monetary historian, established that the value of money is not intrinsic but relational — determined by the ratio between the volume of money and the volume of goods and services it circulates against. When banks create money faster than the economy creates value, the ratio shifts. Prices rise. Wages do not keep pace. The gap between earnings and house prices — the gap the FAIR-Index measures — is a consequence of this ratio moving in one direction for forty years.

Third: compound interest is a thermodynamic impossibility. Frederick Soddy, the Nobel laureate in chemistry, argued that a financial system based on compound interest demands infinite growth from a finite planet. The mathematics of compound interest and the physics of entropy are irreconcilable. One of them is wrong. The universe is not wrong.

These three propositions — credit creation, money as ratio, compound interest as impossibility — form the analytical backbone of everything Lewis has built. The FAIR-Index measures the consequences. The housing platform addresses them. The novel satirises them. The poetry mourns them.

The ration book, Lewis observes, was honest. It said: there is this much bread, and it will be divided this way, and everyone will get some. The monetary system says: there is unlimited credit, and it will be allocated by banks, and the allocation will be called a market. The ration book was honest because it acknowledged scarcity. The monetary system is dishonest because it manufactures scarcity from abundance. Foster Lewis's Worthington's pale ale, still arriving monthly from the brewery decades after his retirement, was a ration from an honest system. It came. It was real. Nobody pretended it was something other than what it was.

## VII. The Bounce-Back

Lewis moved to Sweden in 2010 with his partner Johanna Olsson, a classically trained clarinettist and conductor, and their two children. He took up guitar seriously — building a Martin 00018 at a Sanden guitar course in Komstad as a fiftieth birthday present from the family — and he began to write in earnest.

The writing produced the FAIR-Index: a reproducible forward indicator of UK housing affordability regimes, built on twenty-seven years of canonical data running from January 1999 to the present, fully open-source, version-controlled under the W-Anchor Protocol. The W-Anchor Protocol was born at two in the morning during a session when the build drifted and the outputs no longer matched the sources. Lewis, the surveyor, built a verification system: every output timestamped, every source checksummed, every claim independently verifiable. The W-Anchor Protocol is the check-weighman's scales applied to a publishing pipeline. It ensures that the measurement on the page corresponds to the measurement in the data. Deuteronomy 25:13, applied to git.

The core finding of the FAIR-Index is that the UK housing crisis is not a supply shortage. It is a credit architecture problem. Basel III macroprudential transmission — the mechanism by which international banking regulations are translated into domestic mortgage lending conditions — structurally excludes the marginal borrower. The first-time buyer. The key worker. The nurse, the teacher, the bus driver. The person the city cannot function without. William Gale of the British Home Group confirmed what the numbers showed: the UK housing market "only really works if new buyers keep paying higher and higher prices." The FAIR-Index measures the inflection points where paying higher prices ceases to be possible.

Five clean repositories — hom-ixFAIRindex, DoughForge, the reproducible self-publishing kit, the Father Brown novella, and the autobiography — house the entire corpus. The DoughForge pipeline builds the books. The FAIR-Index pipeline builds the evidence. The W-Anchor Protocol ensures they correspond.

In November 2025, Home@ix Ltd — the housing delivery platform Lewis had been designing since 2020 — acquired Potton, the fifty-year heritage timber frame brand, from Kingspan. Cawdor Gardens, twenty-five homes in Ross-on-Wye, is the first live deployment. The surveyor who measured Docklands for profit now measures housing affordability for justice. The theodolite has not changed. What changed is where it is pointed.

## VIII. The Ground State

Coleridge wrote: "Work without Hope draws nectar in a sieve, / And Hope without an object cannot live." Lewis quotes this often. "The object is shelter," he says. "The object is dignity." The object is a housing market that works for the people who need houses, not only for the people who own them.

He does not claim to have solved the housing crisis. He claims to have measured it accurately, built a platform that addresses it structurally, and published the evidence so that others can verify the measurement and extend the work. The FAIR-Index is open-source. The methodology is auditable. The data is canonical. The pipeline is reproducible. The receipts are in the repo.

In 2019, Lewis wrote a testament he called "When The Time Comes" — a document addressed to an unspecified future reader, containing the question he wanted to be judged by: "Did I embrace the Love in my heart and the empathy in my soul over the fear and the anger which challenged my sense of right and wrong?"

The question is not rhetorical. It is a measurement. The theodolite pointed inward. The check-weighman weighing his own coal.

"Today is always the starting point," Lewis says. The phrase is a refusal of nostalgia and a refusal of despair simultaneously. The past is evidence, not destiny. The future is a construction site, not a forecast. Jerusalem is always under construction. The work continues. The forge is hot. The bread is daily.

As William Godwin wrote, via *Caleb Williams*: "Tomorrow to fresh woods and pastures new." The woods are fresh because they have not yet been measured. The pastures are new because the theodolite has not yet been pointed at them. The surveyor picks up the instrument. The day begins.
