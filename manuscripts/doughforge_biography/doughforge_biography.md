# Roger G. Lewis: The DoughForge Biography

*From Llantrisant to Docklands to the Valley to the Forge*

---

Roger Glyndwr Lewis was born in Llantrisant, Wales, into a family shaped by coal and chapel. His paternal grandfather, Foster Lewis, was a miner in the Rhondda Valley who spent his working life underground and his retirement writing a memoir called *The Miner's Tale*. His maternal grandfather, Llewellyn Harrison, came from the same communities — men who, as Roger would later write, "having neutralised the worst excesses of one Owning Class, would be horrified that the country is now in the grip of an Owning Class much worse than their forebears."

The memoir was typed by Roger's elder sister Suzanne and sent back and forth between Oxfordshire and Talbot Green. Grampy's wife Doris read to him because his eyesight was failing. Dr Hwyel Francis at the South Wales Miners' Library helped with the photographs. It was a family production, made with the cooperative instinct that had sustained the mining communities through everything the twentieth century had thrown at them.

Roger grew up not in the Valleys but in Barford St Michael, a small village in Oxfordshire where he attended Warriner School in Bloxham. He gardened for Mrs Stapleton at the big house, played village cricket, and attended the first Fairport Convention concert at Broughton Castle. The formative summers — the last one falling in 1980, the year of the Bristol and Brixton riots — gave him an Oxfordshire accent and a Cider with Rosie education in a landscape that felt permanent and was not.

In the summer of 1980, aged sixteen, his family moved to West Germany. His father worked for the government, looking after the training ranges at Saltau. Roger attended Kent School in Rheindahlen, a military boarding school, where his head of sixth form, Mr Harris, found it amusing to have him read Laurie Lee to the assembly in his Oxfordshire vowels. It was there he met Nicola Evans, whom he would later marry.

He returned to the UK just after his eighteenth birthday, in October 1982, to find a very changed country.

---

## The Surveyor

Roger trained as a chartered surveyor — the Valuation branch of the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors. His training firms were Shell UK, Prudential Assurance, London Shop PLC, and Hillier Parker May & Rowden. His final-year thesis was on the Contractors Principle of Valuation for the taxation of gas terminals in Scotland, written during a placement with one of the Seven Sisters oil companies.

He was elected a Professional Associate of the RICS on 14 December 1990. He has been self-employed since that date.

A surveyor's training teaches measurement. A theodolite reads horizontal and vertical angles to a precision of one arc-second. A valuation reads the market to the precision the Red Book requires. The instruments are honest. They tell you the angle between where you are and where you are looking. They do not tell you whether you are looking at the right thing.

Roger spent the next decade looking at Docklands.

---

## Docklands

The RLD Group was founded in 1990. Roger Lewis was its principal. Over the following decade, the group completed hundreds of transactions in the London Docklands property market, totalling over three million square feet of property. The offices were on the Isle of Dogs. The team was small by design.

The flagship project was East 14.com — "The Networked Village," a mixed-use waterside development opposite the Millennium Dome, adjacent to East India Dock. Phase 1 was 42 Orchard Place, a live-work conversion. The plan included datacentres (200,000 and 225,000 square feet), a residential tower, and apartments. It was, twenty years before the term existed, a Dashboard concept built in bricks and mortar — a platform for living and working that integrated technology, community, and supply chain in a single development.

Roger also led a consortium bid to repurpose the Millennium Dome as an entertainment venue — essentially the concept that would eventually become The O2. The vision was right. The timing was right. The consortium had the capability. What it did not have was the political connections. A *News of the World* hit piece characterised the bid as a "strip club and den of iniquity." Tony's cronies got the deal instead. The venue that Roger envisaged was built by someone else, under a different name, and it became the most successful entertainment arena in the world. Roger has the receipts. David, who made the original CGIs for East 14.com and conducted the 2021 Home@ix video interview, is a witness to both the Dome bid and the Docklands years.

East 14.com was the proof of concept that nobody recognised at the time. A mixed-use, technology-integrated, community-structured waterside development — where living, working, and making happened in the same place, connected by a digital infrastructure that didn't yet have a name. Twenty years later, the name arrived: it was a Dashboard. The Home@ix Dashboard is East 14 rebuilt on a modern stack, applied not to a single Docklands site but to a national housing platform.

By the age of thirty, Roger Lewis was a millionaire. By thirty-eight, he had retired from the London Docklands market, having banked substantial profits accumulated from 1989 through to 2003.

"My sole objective was very narrow," he wrote later. "I wanted solely to make money and get out. I really didn't enjoy the experience. It made me very selfish and ultimately very ill. My business career showed me the worst of human greed in myself."

---

## The Valley

In 2003, Roger sold his main company. His world fell apart.

He and Nicola divorced. He became, in his own words, a drug-addicted alcoholic. He spent two years trying to self-destruct. He attempted suicide. He was in private therapy for over ten years. He was diagnosed with bipolar disorder.

"It was a very lonely time," he wrote, "and if not for my wealth at that time I think I would have sunk without trace."

He bought the Belmont Estate near Bristol — a country house adjoining Tyntesfield — and became what he called an eccentric country squire. The Rolls-Royce and the Bentley sat in the garage among other automotive ego props acquired in the aftermath of divorce. The dream that had sustained him since gardening for Mrs Stapleton — the dream of owning the big house — had come true, and it was empty.

This is the valley. Not a metaphor. The valley of the shadow of death, walked through by a man who had measured three million square feet of London property and could not measure the distance between wealth and life.

---

## Faith and Fatherhood

In 2005, his daughter Rhiannon was born.

"When I became a father I cleaned up and found a sense of purpose and responsibility I had never felt before. Since 2005 when my daughter was born I have become the person I always was inside."

In 2008, he met Johanna Olsson — a classically trained clarinettist, conductor, and composer. Their son Rasmus was born in 2009. In January 2010, the family moved to Sweden, to the hills outside Angelholm in Skåne, where Roger set up a small recording studio and began to write.

Meanwhile, the financial crisis had reached the Belmont Estate. Yorkshire Bank, through its parent Clydesdale, appointed Law of Property Act receivers. By April 2011, the mansion and the estate lands had been sold at undervalue. By 2012, the carriage house — where the children had been born — was repossessed. The surveyor who had valued three million square feet of other people's property watched his own estate sold from under him by the instruments he had been trained to trust.

All glory to Jesus Christ my Saviour.

Roger does not say this as a formality. He says it because a man who walked through the valley and came out the other side knows what carried him. The redemption is not a chapter in a self-help book. It is the load-bearing wall of everything that follows.

---

## The Forge

On 15 April 2011, Roger published his first blog post: "Guitar as Therapy: Mine Saves Me from the Greedy Bank." Thirteen days later, he published a letter to a friend that was really a letter to everyone — a confession, an accounting, a witness statement from the valley floor.

The blog became *Not The Grub Street Journal* — fifteen years of writing, hundreds of posts, a corpus of economic analysis, poetry, and philippics that mapped the architecture of extraction from the inside. The man who had made millions extracting value from Docklands was now documenting how the same system extracted value from everyone else.

He took up guitar seriously. He attended a Sanden guitar-building course in Komstad, Sweden — a fiftieth birthday present from Johanna, Rhiannon, and Rasmus. He built a Martin 00018 over two weeks with his own hands. A man who had built an estate agency, a development group, and a country estate now built a guitar. The scale changed. The discipline did not.

The blog became the workshop. Post by post, year by year, the analysis sharpened. The monetary theory that began as intuition — a surveyor's gut feeling that the scales were rigged — acquired formal structure. Irving Fisher. Frederick Soddy. Richard Werner's credit creation theory. Wynne Godley's sectoral balances. The man who had valued oil refineries and gas terminals for the Seven Sisters was now applying the same analytical discipline to the monetary system itself.

The writing led to the FAIR-Index — a reproducible forward indicator of UK housing affordability, built on twenty-seven years of canonical data, fully open-source, version-controlled under the W-Anchor Protocol. The core finding: the housing crisis is not a supply shortage. It is a credit architecture problem. Basel III macroprudential transmission structurally excludes the marginal borrower — the first-time buyer, the key worker, the person the city cannot function without.

The FAIR-Index is a check-weighman's instrument. Foster Lewis, Roger's grandfather, worked in a colliery where the miners elected a check-weighman to stand at the company's scales and verify the weight of coal. The colliery had one set of scales. The miners had theirs. Deuteronomy 25:13: *Do not have two differing weights in your bag.* The FAIR-Index is not provided by the housing market. It is not funded by the mortgage lenders. It is built by the measured, carried to the point of measurement, and it reads the scales itself.

The check-weighman's grandson built the FAIR-Index. He did not set out to. He set out to build houses. The scales were wrong.

## Home@ix

On 12 November 2025, Home@ix Ltd acquired Potton from Kingspan Timber Solutions. Potton was established in 1964, the same year Roger was born. For fifty years, Potton had been the most innovative party in every deal and the least rewarded — designing homes, creating Plot Passports, manufacturing and erecting timber frames for master developers who captured all the value. Home@ix exists to fix that.

The acquisition was the culmination of a thesis five years in the making. In June 2021, Roger recorded a video explaining the Home@ix concept: "Home@ix is a dashboard. It puts the customer in the driving seat. By definition, we build to order." The video has 451 views. The concept it describes is now a live deployment.

The platform inverts the deal structure. At Tadpole Garden Village in North Swindon — a 1,855-home Crest Nicholson flagship — Potton had been the specialist partner for fourteen custom-build plots. Potton designed the Concept Homes, created the Plot Passports, manufactured and erected the timber frames. Crest Nicholson held the land, the planning, the infrastructure, and all the upside. Potton held a subcontract and a fee for services. Home@ix exists to invert that equation. The Plot Passport is no longer a service provided to a master developer. It is a platform tool that Home@ix deploys to create and capture value — from land identification to keys in the door. Three brand tiers serve three markets: Stylem@ix for key workers and first-time buyers, Superm@ix for families, Ultim@ix for premium bespoke. No senior development debt. Customer-funded at plot level. The financial architecture is as important as the physical architecture.

The first live deployment is Cawdor Gardens in Ross-on-Wye — twenty-five homes, six affordable, offsite-built, carbon-tracked, Plot Passport structured. Manufacturing partner: Oakwrights, thirty minutes from site. Projected returns: twenty-five percent on gross development value, thirty-four percent on cost.

Steve Baker, at the time of the acquisition: "The housing market is broken. Home@ix is here to fix it."

## DoughForge

DoughForge is the name Roger gives to the publishing and intellectual infrastructure behind everything. Bread and homes. Daily bread and daily shelter. The two most fundamental human needs. *Give us this day our daily bread* is not a metaphor when you are building a forge.

The DoughForge corpus is fifty-five chapters, fifty-five thousand words, four reading tracks, three books, and a Flux Capacitor widget that detects bias the way the check-weighman detected dishonest scales. It contains the FAIR-Index thesis, the Father Brown detective novel, the Shandean autobiography, the Pete and Dud dialogues, the poetry, the philippics, and the complete transclusion register that maps every source to every claim.

The surveyor who measured Docklands for profit now measures affordability for justice. The instruments are the same. The theodolite has not changed. What changed is where it is pointed.

The forge is hot. The dough is on the counter. The bread is daily.
