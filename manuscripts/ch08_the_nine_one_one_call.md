# Chapter 8: The Nine-One-One Call

*In which Father Brown considers the difference between an emergency
and an inconvenience, and discovers that the distinction depends
entirely on who is being inconvenienced.*

---

"Imagine," said Father Brown, "that your house is on fire."

"I'd rather not," said Flambeau, who had once set fire to a
warehouse in Marseilles and still felt guilty about it.

"Imagine it nonetheless. Your house is on fire. You call the fire
brigade. The fire brigade answers. And they say: 'Thank you for
calling. Your call is important to us. A fire safety officer will
be assigned to your case within five to seven working days. In the
meantime, please consult our online FAQ for tips on living in a
burning building.'"

Flambeau said nothing.

"That," said Father Brown, "is what happened to Roger."

---

## The Emergency That Wasn't

Roger's website was his business. Not a hobby. Not a vanity project.
A business. When his domain was redirected to a parking page, his
customers could not find him. His emails may have been affected. His
professional reputation — built over years — was replaced, in the
eyes of anyone who typed his web address, with a page suggesting his
domain was abandoned and available for purchase.

In any reasonable system, this would be treated as an emergency. A
domain hijack — even an accidental one — is the digital equivalent
of someone changing the locks on your shop and putting up a sign
saying "CLOSED — UNDER NEW MANAGEMENT."

But it was not treated as an emergency.

It was treated as a *ticket*.

---

## The Theology of Urgency

"There is a theology of urgency," said Father Brown, settling into his
chair with the particular deliberation of a man who has been thinking
about this for some time and has decided that the chair deserves to know.
"Every institution has one. It is never written down. It is more
fundamental than any policy document, more binding than any terms of
service. It is the institution's private, unspoken answer to the question:
*whose emergency counts?*"

He held up one finger.

"A bank's servers go down. Engineers are woken at three in the morning.
Executives are briefed within the hour. A war room is opened. The
incident has a severity level, a response team, a communications
protocol, an estimated time to resolution that is measured in minutes
and reported to the board. This is an emergency. This is treated as an
emergency because the bank's *own* operations are affected."

He held up a second finger.

"A small business owner's domain is hijacked. His customers cannot find
him. His revenue stops. His reputation is being actively damaged with
every passing hour. He submits a support ticket. The ticket enters a
queue. It is assigned a priority level — low, because the system that
assigns priority levels does not know and does not ask what the domain
is *for*. The ticket is addressed in the order it was received, behind
password resets and billing queries and a man in Doncaster who cannot
remember his username."

He put his hand down.

"The house burns," said Flambeau, "at the speed of the queue."

"The house burns," Father Brown agreed, "and the fire brigade arrives
in five to seven working days, surveys the ashes, and closes the ticket
as *resolved — no active fire detected*."

---

## The Menu

Roger's attempt to escalate — to find the emergency number, the human
voice, the person with authority to act — was, Father Brown explained,
a study in architectural futility.

"Tell me what happened," said Flambeau. "Step by step."

Father Brown produced a sheet of paper on which he had transcribed, in
his careful hand, the sequence of Roger's attempts to reach a human
being with the power to fix his problem in real time. He read it aloud
with the measured cadence of a man reading a particularly instructive
passage of scripture.

"Step one. Roger calls the support number listed on the registrar's
website. He reaches an automated menu. The menu offers four options:
billing, new registrations, technical support, and 'other.' He presses
three for technical support."

"Step two. The technical support line offers three sub-options: DNS
configuration, email forwarding, and 'general enquiry.' His domain has
been hijacked, which is not DNS configuration in the way the menu means
it. He presses three for general enquiry."

"Step three. A recorded message informs him that due to 'unprecedented
demand,' wait times are currently forty-five to sixty minutes. He is
invited to visit the online help centre, where — the recording assures
him — most queries can be resolved without speaking to an agent."

"Step four. He waits fifty-three minutes. A human answers. The human
asks for his account number, his domain name, his postcode, and the
last four digits of the card on file. He provides all of these. The
human says: 'I can see the domain is resolving correctly at this time.'"

"Step five. Roger explains that the domain *was* redirected. That he
has screenshots. That this is not a current-state question but a
what-happened question. The human says: 'I understand your frustration.
Unfortunately, I can only assist with current technical issues. For
historical queries, you would need to submit a written request to our
investigations team.'"

"Step six. Roger asks for the email address of the investigations
team. The human provides a generic address:
*investigations@[registrar].com*."

"Step seven. Roger sends a detailed email to that address. He includes
screenshots, timestamps, a technical description of the redirect, and
a clear statement of what he is asking for: an explanation of how and
why his domain was redirected."

"Step eight." Father Brown paused. "There is no step eight. The email
was never answered."

---

## The Silence After the Menu

Flambeau, who in his criminal days had broken into vaults, cracked
safes, and once stolen an entire Matisse from a gallery in Nice while
the security guard was in the lavatory, sat very still.

"No reply at all?"

"No reply at all. Not an acknowledgement. Not an auto-response. Not a
ticket number. Not even a 'thank you for reaching out, we appreciate
your patience.' Nothing. The email entered the address and was not
heard from again, like a message in a bottle thrown into a sea that
had been concreted over."

"That's —"

"That is the architecture," said Father Brown.

He said it quietly. He always said the important things quietly. The
devastating sermons were delivered at the volume of a lullaby, and
the congregation leaned forward to hear them, which was — Father Brown
had always believed — the proper posture for receiving bad news.

"Look at what is missing from the interaction. There is no ticket
number. There is no reference code that Roger can cite in a follow-up.
There is no named officer. There is no callback. There is no
escalation path — no option to say 'this is urgent, please treat it
as urgent.' The menu does not contain an option for 'my business is
being damaged right now.' The system does not have a category for
the user's emergency. It has categories for its own operations —
billing, new registrations, technical configuration — and a catch-all
called 'other' that leads to a queue that leads to a human who can
only address the present tense."

---

## The Surveyor's Comparison

Father Brown reached for his tea. It was cold. He drank it anyway,
because penitence is a habit and cold tea is a minor penance that
keeps the larger penances in perspective.

"Consider, by contrast, how a different institution handles a
different complaint."

He produced a second sheet of paper. This one was typed. It was a
complaint form from the Land Registry of England and Wales — the body
responsible for the definitive record of property ownership in the
jurisdiction.

"Roger is a chartered surveyor. He has dealt with the Land Registry
for thirty-five years. If a title register contains an error — if
someone's property boundary has been incorrectly recorded, or if a
charge has been registered against the wrong title — the process is
as follows."

He read.

"The complainant submits form OC1, citing the title number and the
nature of the discrepancy. Within three working days, a caseworker
is assigned. The caseworker has a name, an extension number, and a
reference code. The complainant can telephone the caseworker directly.
If the matter is urgent — and the form includes a box for indicating
urgency — the response time is reduced to twenty-four hours. If the
matter involves potential fraud, it is escalated to the Land Registry's
Counter-Fraud team, which has a dedicated telephone line and a named
head of department."

He put the paper down.

"The Land Registry is not a perfect institution. It makes errors. Its
caseworkers are sometimes slow, sometimes wrong, sometimes
bureaucratically obtuse. But it has a *process*. It has escalation.
It has named officers. It has a definition of 'urgent' that is
determined by the *complainant's* situation, not by the institution's
operational convenience. And it has this because it was designed in an
era when the people who designed institutions believed that the
institution existed to serve the public, not the other way around."

"And the domain registrar?"

"The domain registrar was designed in an era when the people who
designed institutions believed that the institution existed to
achieve scale. And at scale, the individual complaint is not a
signal. It is noise. The architecture is optimised to reduce noise.
The queue is noise reduction. The menu is noise reduction. The
auto-response is noise reduction. The silence — the email that
vanishes into the void — is the most efficient noise reduction of
all."

---

## The Pivot

Flambeau watched Father Brown carefully. He had known the priest
for many years, and he recognised the particular quality of
stillness that preceded a change in direction — the moment when
the investigation stopped being about what had happened and
started being about what it *meant*.

"Roger stopped being angry," said Father Brown.

"When?"

"During step five. When the human on the telephone said 'I can only
assist with current technical issues.' That was the moment. I have
read his notes — he keeps a journal, you know, a physical notebook,
the kind a surveyor uses in the field. And the entry for that day
changes. The early entries are frustrated. Emphatic. Underlined.
The entry after the phone call is different. It is measured. Precise.
The handwriting is smaller. The sentences are shorter."

"He calmed down?"

"He did something more dangerous than calming down. He became a
surveyor."

Father Brown stood and walked to the window. The view from the
vestry was of the churchyard, where headstones of varying antiquity
stood in the rain with the patience of people who have learned that
complaining about the weather is futile.

"An angry man writes a complaint. A surveyor writes a *record*. The
difference is purpose. The complaint asks the institution to fix
something. The record documents that the institution will not. The
complaint depends on the institution's goodwill. The record does not
depend on anything except ink and paper and the willingness to write
down what happened, in what order, with what timestamps."

"He started gathering evidence."

"He started gathering evidence. Not for a lawsuit — he knew by step
five that no lawsuit would follow, because the damages were difficult
to quantify and the costs of litigation would exceed them. Not for a
regulatory complaint — he knew by step eight that the regulators
were part of the architecture he was complaining about. He started
gathering evidence because that is what a surveyor does when the
instruments show that the ground is not where the map says it should
be. You do not argue with the ground. You do not petition the ground.
You measure it, you record it, and you publish the measurement."

---

## The Absence as Finding

"And here," said Father Brown, returning to his chair, "is the
thing I have been trying to say for the last three chapters."

Flambeau raised an eyebrow. "You've been counting?"

"The thing I have been trying to say is this: the absence of an
escalation path is not an oversight. It is a finding."

He let this settle.

"When a surveyor examines a building and discovers that there is
no fire escape, he does not need to prove that the building's owner
intended to trap the occupants. He does not need to demonstrate
malice. He needs only to note: *there is no fire escape*. The
absence is the evidence. The absence is the violation. The building
code requires a fire escape. The building does not have one. The
surveyor records this and moves on."

"And Roger's domain registrar?"

"Roger's domain registrar has no emergency number. No named officer
for hijacked domains. No escalation path for business-critical
failures. No acknowledgement protocol for investigation requests.
No definition of 'urgent' that includes the user's situation."

Father Brown picked up his cold tea again, looked at it, and set
it down.

"You do not need to prove that the scorpion is malicious. The
scorpion stings because it must, and the support architecture
fails because it was built to fail — not through conspiracy but
through the simple, structural fact that the user's emergency was
never part of the design specification. The frog drowns either
way. The only question is whether anyone records the depth of
the water."

He paused.

"Roger recorded the depth of the water. That is what the next
chapter is about."

---

## The Complaint Becomes the Evidence

Flambeau stood, stretched, and walked to the tea things. He poured
two fresh cups — hot this time, because he felt that the
investigation had earned it.

"So the complaint failed," he said.

"The complaint succeeded," said Father Brown. "Not as a complaint.
As evidence. Every unanswered email is a data point. Every
redirect between departments is a measurement. Every 'we can only
assist with current issues' is a surveyor's reading that says: the
institution cannot see the past and has no mechanism for seeing it.
The timestamps are the evidence. The ticket numbers that don't
exist are the evidence. The silence is the evidence."

He accepted the hot tea with the gratitude of a man who has been
drinking cold tea as a spiritual exercise and is quietly relieved
that someone has intervened.

"A surveyor walks onto a site and measures what is there. But a
good surveyor also measures what is *not* there. The missing fire
escape. The absent damp-proof course. The load-bearing wall that
the drawings show but the building doesn't have. Roger's complaint
file is a survey of absence. It documents, with the precision of
a man trained to measure buildings, every point at which an
institution that was supposed to protect him had no floor, no wall,
and no door."

He sipped the tea. It was hot. It was adequate.

"The nine-one-one call," he said, "was never going to connect.
The system has no emergency number. But the record of the call —
the forty-five-minute wait, the eight steps, the vanished email,
the silence — that record is more damning than any emergency
response could have been. Because an institution that fixes your
problem can say it was an anomaly. An institution that has no
mechanism for hearing your problem cannot say anything at all."

He smiled. The small, dangerous smile that Flambeau had learned,
over many years, to recognise as the expression of a priest who
has arrived at a verdict.

"Shall we proceed to the verdict?"

"I thought you'd never ask, Rev."

---

*End of Chapter 8*
