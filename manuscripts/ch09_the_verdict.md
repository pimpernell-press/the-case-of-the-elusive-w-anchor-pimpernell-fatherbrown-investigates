# Chapter 9: The Verdict

*In which Father Brown delivers his judgement, Flambeau pours the
last of the tea, and the guilty are named — though not, perhaps,
in the way anyone expected.*

---

The tea had gone cold for the third time. Father Brown did not seem
to mind. He had, over the course of this investigation, consumed
more cold tea than hot, which Flambeau suspected was less a matter
of absent-mindedness than a deliberate spiritual discipline — a
small, continuous penance for the sins he was about to describe.

"Let me tell you what happened," said Father Brown.

He did not raise his voice. He never raised his voice. The most
devastating sermons, he had always believed, were delivered at
the volume of a lullaby.

---

## The Crime

"A man registered a domain. He paid for it. He used it for his
business. One day, without his knowledge or consent, the domain
was redirected to a page that suggested it was abandoned. This
page generated advertising revenue for the company that operated
it — the same company that, through a chain of acquisitions, was
supposed to be *managing* the domain on the man's behalf.

"When the man complained, he was directed in circles between
organisations that all denied responsibility. When the problem
was fixed — before he had even finished complaining — the fix
was used as evidence that no problem had ever existed. When he
asked for an explanation, he was told that no explanation was
necessary because the problem was resolved. When he asked for
accountability, he was told that accountability was not within
the scope of the complaints process.

"The man was not robbed at gunpoint. He was robbed by *process*.
By terms and conditions. By corporate restructuring. By a system
designed so that no single person, at any single point, did
anything that looked — in isolation — like wrongdoing."

---

## The Guilty

"And so," said Father Brown, "who is guilty?"

He held up his fingers.

"**Heart Internet** is guilty — of selling a service and then
selling the company that provided it, without ensuring that the
new owners would honour the original promise. They sold Roger's
trust along with their servers, and they did not ask Roger's
permission.

"**GoDaddy** is guilty — of operating a system in which domains
managed on behalf of customers could be redirected to revenue-
generating parking pages. Whether this was deliberate policy or
negligent architecture, the effect was the same: they profited
from a service failure that harmed their customer.

"**Nominet** is guilty — of maintaining a regulatory framework
that protects registrars more than registrants. Of operating a
complaints process designed to exhaust rather than resolve. Of
being, in the most literal sense, a *gatekeeper* that has
forgotten it is supposed to be guarding the gate on behalf of
the people *outside*, not the people *inside*.

"And all three are guilty of the greatest sin available to an
institution: the sin of making the victim feel that they are the
problem."

---

## The Penance

Flambeau waited. In the old days — the criminal days — this would
be the moment he'd ask, "So what do we steal?" But he was reformed
now, and Father Brown did not steal things. Father Brown did
something worse. He *explained* things. In public. With footnotes.

"The penance," said Father Brown, "is transparency. The record
must exist. The complaint must be documented. The timeline must be
published. Not because it will change what happened — nothing can
change what happened — but because the next Roger must be able to
find this story and know that he is not mad. That the circles are
real. That the timestamps don't lie. That the epilogue was written
first."

He stood up, collected his umbrella, and put on his hat — a hat
so shapeless and apologetic that it seemed to be asking forgiveness
for existing.

"The Church," he said, "has survived two thousand years of scandal
by one method alone: someone, somewhere, always wrote it down.
The internet should be no different."

"And Roger?" asked Flambeau.

Father Brown smiled.

"Roger," he said, "has written it down."

On Flambeau's phone, abandoned face-up on the table, a notification
pulsed. A Substack post. The thumbnail showed Melvyn Bragg in his
studio, books stacked behind him. The title read: *THE SOUTH BANK
SHOW SPECIAL: "The Trial of Usury — In Which the Poets Gather to
Judge the Inter-Alpha Group."*

Neither of them noticed.
