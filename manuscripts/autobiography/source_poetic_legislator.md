# Foreword

Foreword

In this Book I have sought to explain myself doing that I think the Foreword to my Grandfathers memoir is appropriate.

Dedication

To the Miners of South Wales

First printed for the family in 1985, I remember my sister Suzanne typing early drafts, which were sent back and forth from Oxfordshire to Talbot Green. My Cousin Russel mentioned one evening when we met for a drink in Clifton Village, Bristol in summer 2022 that he had a copy of Grampy’s book. I read a chapter aloud later that evening, and was transported back to long evenings sat at the dining table with Nanny listening to the Television and Grampy and Dad chatting away with a glass of Worthington’s pale ale, from the ration still sent every month from the brewery.

Written 40 years ago now, I wonder what Grampy would make of the present discontents.

I am sure he and those of his generation, including my maternal grandfather Llewellyn Harrison, would be horrified that having neutralised the worst excesses of one “Owning Class,” it would appear that the country is now in the grip of an Owning Class much worse than their forebears.

I thought I should retype and include Grampy’s memoir as part of the collection of the Going Direct Spring 2024 trilogy.

‘A thousand lie beneath the sky,

They rot beneath the sun,

And we who live shall not forgive

This deed your hand hath done!’

Baards of Wales. Janos Arany

BOOK I: ORIGINS - THE MINER’S TALE

Thursday Summary: Industrial Heritage and the Making of Consciousness

The story begins not with birth, but with the deep geological time of coal seams and the men who descended into darkness to bring light to the world above. Foster Lewis’s “The Miner’s Tale” serves as our foundational text, for it is in the mines of South Wales that we find the first formation of a consciousness that would later grapple with the abstract mechanisms of modern finance.

In the opening pages of Foster’s memoir, we encounter a world where value was tangible—coal hewn from rock, sweat crystallized into wages, community forged in the crucible of shared danger. The miners’ cooperative societies, their mutual aid associations, their understanding that individual survival depended on collective solidarity—these were the first lessons in alternative economics, taught not in university lecture halls but in the lamplight of underground chambers.

The biographical subject of our study inherits this tradition not through direct lineage but through cultural osmosis, through the stories that permeate the valleys and the songs that echo in the pubs. The transition from physical extraction to financial abstraction becomes the central tension of the intellectual journey we are tracing.

Poetic Companions: Blake’s Jerusalem and Morris’s Socialist Vision

William Blake’s “Jerusalem” provides our first poetic framework:

“And did those feet in ancient time

Walk upon England’s mountains green?

And was the holy Lamb of God

On England’s pleasant pastures seen?”

But Blake’s vision of England’s green and pleasant land stands in stark contrast to the industrial reality of the mining communities. The “dark Satanic Mills” are not metaphorical but literal—the pit heads and processing plants that transformed landscape and lives.

William Morris extends this vision into practical socialism, his “News from Nowhere” offering an alternative to industrial capitalism that resonates with the cooperative traditions of the mining communities. Morris’s understanding that true wealth lies in meaningful work and community bonds, not in the accumulation of abstract capital, becomes a guiding principle.

Biographical Arc: The Formation of Economic Consciousness

The young consciousness being formed in this environment learns several crucial lessons:

The Labor Theory of Value: Value comes from human effort applied to natural resources

Collective Solidarity: Individual welfare depends on community strength

Suspicion of Abstraction: Real wealth is tangible, measurable, useful

Cyclical Thinking: Boom and bust are natural rhythms, not aberrations

These lessons, learned not from books but from lived experience and inherited wisdom, become the foundation for later critiques of financial capitalism. When our subject encounters the abstractions of modern banking—derivatives, securitization, quantitative easing—the response is visceral: this is not real wealth but elaborate fiction.

The Legislators’ Assembly: Blake, Morris, and the Radical Tradition

Blake: “The imagination is not a state: it is the human existence itself. Your miners understood this—they imagined a world beyond the darkness of the pit, and in that imagination lay the seeds of revolution.”

Morris: “But imagination without action is mere fancy. The miners built their cooperatives, their societies, their unions. They understood that the change they sought had to be constructed, brick by brick, meeting by meeting, strike by strike.”

The Subject: “Yet we lost something in the transition from coal to credit. The miners could see their product, touch it, know its worth. How do we apply their wisdom to a world where wealth is numbers on screens?”

Blake: “By remembering that behind every abstraction lies a human reality. Every derivative represents someone’s home, someone’s labor, someone’s life. The task is to make visible the invisible.”

Morris: “And by building alternatives. The miners didn’t just critique the mine owners—they created their own institutions. What are the modern equivalents of their cooperatives and mutual aid societies?”

This dialogue establishes the central tension of our biography: how to apply the concrete wisdom of industrial solidarity to the abstract challenges of financial capitalism.

THE PLANTSMAN:PRECOGNITIVE POSTCARDS A Prologue

A Prologue in Three Chapters

Being Postcards from a Journey to the Unknown, Written Before the Journey Began

PROLOGUE: THE TICKET PURCHASE

In Which One Realizes the Train Has Already Left the Station

I started writing this because I realized I was locked into a series of events for which I would be subject to the results, but over which I would neither be consulted nor considered¹. The realization was quite comforting, in the way that discovering you’re already falling can be comforting—at least the uncertainty is over².

We’re all precogs now³. Not in the Philip K. Dick sense of seeing the future, but in the more mundane sense of recognizing patterns that haven’t quite finished repeating themselves⁴. The postcards I’m writing are from a journey that began before I bought the ticket, to destinations that were selected before I knew I was traveling⁵.

This blog represents a purchase—mentally—for my ticket for the ride⁶. A journey I was determined to understand, enjoy, and share some postcards with myself and others who might be interested, having realized they too may be taking the same train⁷. As with all postcards, some are written more thoughtfully, others are scribbles just checking in or marking out places to revisit⁸.

The train, I’ve discovered, runs on a peculiar schedule⁹. It stops at stations that don’t appear on any official map: Artificial Scarcity Junction, Planned Obsolescence Central, and the sprawling terminus of Systematic Abundance Suppression¹⁰. The conductors speak in languages that sound like economics but mean something entirely different¹¹.

The Integral Compartment

In Which All Quadrants Lead to the Same Destination

The train car I found myself in was divided into four sections, each offering a different view of the same landscape¹². In the Upper-Right compartment, passengers observed behaviors and actions—the external manifestations of a system designed to extract maximum value from minimum input¹³. They watched governance structures and geopolitical power dynamics with the detached fascination of people watching a chess game played with living pieces¹⁴.

The Upper-Left section housed those who focused on internal experiences and interpretations¹⁵. These passengers discussed ideologies and beliefs with the intensity of people who had discovered that their personal perspectives were not, in fact, personal¹⁶. They spoke of cultural programming and manufactured consent with the wonder of archaeologists discovering that their civilization had been built on someone else’s foundations¹⁷.

In the Lower-Right compartment, systems analysts mapped the external collective structures¹⁸. They traced the flow of capital, resources, and decision-making power through networks that resembled circulatory systems designed by someone who had never studied biology¹⁹. Global governance regimes, economic systems, and geopolitical power structures were dissected with the precision of surgeons operating on patients they suspected were already dead²⁰.

The Lower-Left section contained the most unsettled passengers²¹. These were the ones who studied collective internal experiences—the shared psychology of societies that had been convinced to participate in their own systematic disadvantage²². They spoke of cultural history as if it were a crime scene, and collective psychology as if it were a form of mass hypnosis that had been so successful that even the hypnotists had forgotten they were performing it²³.

What struck me most was how all four perspectives, despite their different vantage points, kept arriving at the same conclusion²⁴. The train was not taking us where we thought we were going²⁵. The destination had been predetermined by people who were not on the train²⁶. And the ticket we thought we had purchased was actually a subscription to a service we had never agreed to use²⁷.

The Semiotic Signal Box

In Which Signs Point in All Directions Except the Right One

The train’s communication system operated on principles that would have fascinated Ferdinand de Saussure, had he lived to see language weaponized against meaning²⁸. Every announcement contained signifiers that had been systematically divorced from their signifieds²⁹. “Sustainable development” meant unsustainable extraction³⁰. “Global governance” meant local disenfranchisement³¹. “Economic growth” meant systematic wealth transfer³².

The conductors had mastered what I began to think of as “inverse semiotics”³³—the art of using words to mean their opposites while maintaining plausible deniability about the reversal³⁴. They spoke of “multipolarity” while describing systems that concentrated power in fewer hands³⁵. They promoted “autonomy” through mechanisms that increased dependency³⁶. They advocated for “more nations having a say” in processes designed to ensure that saying and doing remained entirely separate activities³⁷.

The most sophisticated passengers had learned to decode these linguistic inversions³⁸. They understood that when officials spoke of “breaking regions into more autonomous supply chains,” they meant creating more points of control and vulnerability³⁹. When “behavioral psychology” was mentioned in policy documents, they knew that manipulation was being rebranded as science⁴⁰. When “sustainable development goals” were promoted, they recognized the creation of human settlements that would be sustainable for everyone except the humans settled in them⁴¹.

But even the decoders faced a problem⁴². The system had evolved beyond simple linguistic inversion⁴³. It now operated on multiple levels of meaning simultaneously, creating what semioticians might call “quantum signification”⁴⁴—words that meant different things depending on who was observing them and from what position⁴⁵.

The train’s destination board exemplified this phenomenon⁴⁶. It displayed “Prosperity Station” to passengers in first class, “Sustainability Central” to those in second class, and “Compliance Terminal” to those in third class⁴⁷. All three displays showed the same arrival time, but only the third one was accurate⁴⁸.

The Hermeneutic Engine

In Which Interpretation Becomes the Only Reality

The train itself, I realized, was powered by interpretation⁴⁹. Not coal, not electricity, not even the renewable energy sources promoted in the passenger brochures⁵⁰. It ran on the collective human capacity to find meaning in meaninglessness, to discover patterns in chaos, and to maintain hope in the face of systematically manufactured despair⁵¹.

The engine room contained what the chief engineer called “the hermeneutic apparatus”⁵²—a complex system for converting human interpretive energy into locomotive power⁵³. Every time a passenger tried to understand what was happening, their mental effort was captured and converted into forward momentum⁵⁴. The more confused people became, the faster the train traveled⁵⁵.

This explained why the journey had become so accelerated in recent years⁵⁶. The multiplication of interpretive frameworks—integral, semiotic, economic, political, spiritual, technological—had created an abundance of hermeneutic fuel⁵⁷. People were working harder than ever to understand their situation, and their efforts were being harvested to power the very systems that kept them confused⁵⁸.

The most efficient fuel came from what the engineers called “interpretive loops”⁵⁹—situations where people’s attempts to understand their circumstances actually prevented them from changing those circumstances⁶⁰. Academic analysis that led to no action⁶¹. Political discourse that generated heat but no light⁶². Spiritual practices that provided inner peace while external conditions deteriorated⁶³. Economic theories that explained why things had to be the way they were⁶⁴.

But I had begun to suspect that the hermeneutic engine had a design flaw⁶⁵. It required people to keep trying to understand, which meant it depended on hope⁶⁶. And hope, as any student of human nature knows, is remarkably persistent⁶⁷. Even when systematically disappointed, it tends to regenerate itself⁶⁸.

The question was whether this regenerative capacity could be harnessed for something other than locomotive power⁶⁹. Whether the same interpretive energy that powered the train toward predetermined destinations could be redirected toward destinations of our own choosing⁷⁰.

The postcards I was writing were an experiment in this possibility⁷¹. Each one was a small act of interpretive rebellion⁷²—an attempt to use the hermeneutic apparatus for navigation rather than propulsion⁷³. To read the landscape we were passing through rather than simply being transported across it⁷⁴.

TRANSITION: THE APPROACHING STATIONS

As I write this, the train is approaching two stations that don’t appear on any official schedule⁷⁵. The first is called “Conquest of Dough”⁷⁶—a place where the ancient human struggle for daily bread has been transformed into a complex financial instrument⁷⁷. The second is “The Clockwork Forest”⁷⁸—a destination where natural systems have been replaced by mechanical ones that simulate nature while extracting value from the simulation⁷⁹.

These stations represent the culmination of the journey I began documenting⁸⁰. They are places where the patterns I’ve been observing reach their logical conclusion⁸¹. Where the interpretive energy that powers our confusion finally encounters the reality it has been working so hard to avoid understanding⁸².

The postcards that follow will be written from these stations⁸³. They will describe what it looks like when human systems become so sophisticated at extracting value that they begin to extract the conditions necessary for their own existence⁸⁴. When the hermeneutic engine becomes so efficient at converting understanding into power that it eliminates the human capacity for understanding⁸⁵.

But they will also describe what happens when people stop trying to understand the system and start trying to understand each other⁸⁶. When interpretive energy is redirected from analysis to action⁸⁷. When the postcards stop being descriptions of where we’ve been and become invitations to where we might go⁸⁸.

The train is slowing down⁸⁹. The next station is approaching⁹⁰. Time to gather my postcards and prepare for whatever comes next⁹¹.

BOOK II: AWAKENING - THE STRAWBERRY CONSPIRACY

Thursday Summary: Natural Abundance vs. Artificial Scarcity

The second movement of our intellectual biography begins with a moment of revelation disguised as a simple observation about fruit production. “The Strawberry Conspiracy” serves as both literal investigation into agricultural economics and metaphorical exploration of how abundance is artificially transformed into scarcity through financial manipulation.

The biographical subject encounters this paradox during a Devon holiday, where a roadside plaque reading “God gave us our family, thank God we can choose our friends” triggers a deeper reflection on the difference between natural gifts and social constructions. The strawberry fields of Devon, capable of producing far more fruit than the market will bear, become a symbol of how economic systems can create scarcity in the midst of plenty.

This awakening to the artificial nature of scarcity marks a crucial transition from accepting economic “laws” as natural phenomena to recognizing them as human constructions that can be challenged and changed.

Poetic Companions: Coleridge’s Nature Mysticism and Shelley’s Revolutionary Vision

Samuel Taylor Coleridge’s understanding of nature as an organic whole provides the philosophical framework for questioning artificial scarcity:

“In Xanadu did Kubla Khan

A stately pleasure-dome decree:

Where Alph, the sacred river, ran

Through caverns measureless to man

Down to a sunless sea.”

Coleridge’s vision of natural abundance—rivers running through measureless caverns—contrasts sharply with the constructed scarcity of economic systems. His concept of organic unity suggests that artificial divisions between abundance and scarcity are violations of natural law.

Percy Bysshe Shelley’s revolutionary idealism provides the political framework:

“Rise like Lions after slumber

In unvanquishable number—

Shake your chains to earth like dew

Which in sleep had fallen on you—

Ye are many—they are few.”

Shelley’s call to recognize the power of the many against the few becomes central to understanding how artificial scarcity is maintained by a small group of financial manipulators against the interests of the abundant many.

Biographical Arc: The Discovery of Economic Contradictions

The strawberry investigation reveals several key contradictions:

Productive Capacity vs. Market Demand: The ability to produce exceeds what the market will purchase

Natural Abundance vs. Financial Scarcity: Nature provides plenty, but financial systems create shortage

Use Value vs. Exchange Value: The utility of goods differs from their market price

Local Production vs. Global Markets: Local abundance is subordinated to global financial flows

These contradictions become the foundation for a broader critique of how financial capitalism distorts natural relationships between production and consumption, abundance and need.

The Devon Holiday Memory: Choosing Our Economic Family

The plaque’s message about choosing friends while accepting family becomes a metaphor for economic relationships. We inherit certain economic structures (family), but we can choose to build alternative relationships (friends) based on mutual aid and cooperation rather than competition and exploitation.

This insight leads to the recognition that economic systems are not natural laws but social choices—we can choose different ways of organizing production and distribution, just as we can choose our friends.

The Legislators’ Assembly: Shelley and Coleridge on Nature and Society

Coleridge: “The strawberry fields reveal what I called the ‘one Life within us and abroad’—the organic unity that connects all natural processes. Your economic systems violate this unity by creating artificial divisions between abundance and scarcity.”

Shelley: “But recognition of the violation is the first step toward revolution. When people see that scarcity is artificially created, they begin to question the authority of those who create it.”

The Subject: “Yet the mechanisms of artificial scarcity have become so complex, so abstract, that most people can’t see the connection between strawberry fields and financial derivatives.”

Coleridge: “This is where the imagination becomes crucial. Poetry, metaphor, symbol—these are the tools for making visible the invisible connections. The strawberry becomes a symbol that people can understand.”

Shelley: “And once they understand, they can act. The task is not just to critique the system but to envision and create alternatives. What would an economy based on natural abundance rather than artificial scarcity look like?”

This dialogue establishes the need for both analytical critique and imaginative vision in challenging economic orthodoxy.

BOOK III: EDUCATION - THE CONQUEST OF DOUGH

Thursday Summary: Monetary Systems and Economic Literacy

The third movement represents the systematic education phase of our intellectual biography. “The Conquest of Dough” serves as both pun and serious investigation into how money—dough—has been conquered by financial elites and how it might be reconquered by democratic forces.

This phase marks the transition from intuitive understanding of economic contradictions to systematic study of monetary theory. The biographical subject encounters the work of economists like Irving Fisher, Frederick Soddy, and contemporary monetary reformers, beginning to understand the technical mechanisms by which money is created and controlled.

The central revelation is that money is not a natural phenomenon but a social technology—and like all technologies, it can be designed to serve different purposes and different groups. The current monetary system serves financial elites; alternative systems could serve the common good.

Poetic Companions: Pound’s Economic Cantos and Graves’s Historical Perspective

Ezra Pound’s economic cantos provide both inspiration and warning. Pound’s understanding of usury as the root of economic evil resonates strongly:

“With usura hath no man a house of good stone

each block cut smooth and well fitting

that design might cover their face,

with usura

hath no man a painted paradise on his church wall”

Pound’s critique of usury—the charging of interest on money created from nothing—becomes central to understanding how the monetary system transfers wealth from productive workers to financial manipulators.

However, Pound’s descent into fascism serves as a warning about the dangers of monetary reform without democratic values. The challenge is to pursue monetary reform within a framework of social justice and democratic participation.

Robert Graves provides historical perspective through his understanding of myth and pattern:

“There is one story and one story only

That will prove worth your telling,

Whether as learned bard or gifted child”

Graves’s recognition that there is “one story”—the eternal struggle between creative and destructive forces—provides framework for understanding monetary reform as part of a larger historical pattern.

Biographical Arc: Economic Awakening and Credit Creation

The systematic study of monetary theory reveals several key insights:

Money Creation: Most money is created by private banks as interest-bearing debt

Debt Slavery: The monetary system creates permanent indebtedness

Democratic Alternatives: Money could be created by public institutions for public benefit

Historical Precedents: Alternative monetary systems have existed and succeeded

This knowledge transforms the biographical subject’s understanding from seeing economic problems as natural disasters to recognizing them as consequences of specific policy choices that could be changed.

The Technical Education: From Intuition to Analysis

The movement from intuitive understanding to technical knowledge represents a crucial development. The subject learns:

How fractional reserve banking works

The difference between money and currency

The role of central banks in monetary policy

Alternative proposals for monetary reform

This technical knowledge provides the tools for effective critique and the foundation for constructive alternatives.

The Legislators’ Assembly: Pound and Graves on Money and History

Pound: “You have grasped the essential point—that usury is the cancer at the heart of civilization. But beware of my mistakes. The enemy is not any particular group of people, but a system that corrupts all who participate in it.”

Graves: “Ezra speaks truly, but incompletely. The usury system is indeed ancient, but so is the resistance to it. Every generation must fight this battle anew, using the tools and understanding of their time.”

The Subject: “But how do we fight a system so complex that most people can’t understand it? How do we make monetary reform a popular cause?”

Pound: “Through clear exposition and vivid imagery. Make the abstract concrete. Show people how the system affects their daily lives—their mortgages, their jobs, their children’s futures.”

Graves: “And through stories. People understand narratives better than theories. Tell the story of how money was captured by private interests and how it can be reclaimed for the common good.”

This dialogue emphasizes the need to combine technical understanding with effective communication strategies.

BOOK IV: CONNECTIONS - THE JAMES BURKE INFLUENCE

Thursday Summary: Systems Thinking and Technological Connections

The fourth movement introduces a crucial influence: James Burke’s approach to understanding technological and social change through interconnected systems rather than isolated events. Burke’s “Connections” series provides a methodology for seeing how apparently unrelated developments combine to create major historical shifts.

This systems thinking becomes essential for understanding how monetary reform connects to broader questions of technology, democracy, and social organization. The biographical subject learns to trace the connections between seemingly separate phenomena—how changes in communication technology affect financial markets, how monetary policy influences political power, how technological innovation can either reinforce or challenge existing hierarchies.

Poetic Companions: Dylan Thomas’s Interconnected Vision

Dylan Thomas provides the poetic framework for understanding interconnection:

“The force that through the green fuse drives the flower

Drives my green age; that blasts the roots of trees

Is my destroyer.

And I am dumb to tell the crooked rose

My youth is bent by the same wintry fever.”

Thomas’s recognition that the same force drives both creation and destruction, growth and decay, provides insight into how technological and financial systems can serve both liberating and oppressive functions depending on how they are organized and controlled.

His vision of cosmic interconnection—where individual experience reflects universal patterns—offers a framework for understanding how personal economic struggles connect to larger systemic issues.

Biographical Arc: Learning to See Systemic Relationships

The Burke influence teaches several key lessons:

Unintended Consequences: Technological changes often have effects far from their original purpose

System Dynamics: Small changes in one part of a system can have large effects elsewhere

Historical Patterns: Similar patterns repeat across different historical periods

Technological Determinism vs. Social Choice: Technology creates possibilities, but social choices determine how they are used

This systems perspective transforms the approach to monetary reform from focusing on isolated policies to understanding how monetary systems connect to technology, politics, culture, and social organization.

The Web of Connections: Technology, Money, and Power

The systems analysis reveals crucial connections:

How digital technology enables new forms of monetary control

How communication networks can either centralize or decentralize power

How financial innovation often serves existing elites rather than creating genuine progress

How alternative technologies could enable more democratic monetary systems

The Legislators’ Assembly: Dylan Thomas on the Web of Existence

Dylan Thomas: “You are beginning to see what I saw—that everything is connected to everything else. The force that drives the financial markets is the same force that drives the flower, but it can be channeled toward creation or destruction.”

The Subject: “But how do we influence such vast, interconnected systems? Individual action seems insignificant against such complexity.”

Thomas: “Remember that you are part of the system, not separate from it. Every action ripples through the web of connections. The question is not whether you have influence, but what kind of influence you choose to exercise.”

James Burke (joining the dialogue): “Dylan is right about interconnection, but we must also understand the specific mechanisms of change. Systems change when key nodes are influenced, when new connections are made, when information flows in new directions.”

The Subject: “So the task is to identify the key nodes and create new connections?”

Burke: “Precisely. In monetary systems, the key nodes are often the points where money is created, where information is controlled, where decisions are made about credit allocation. Change those nodes, and you change the entire system.”

This dialogue establishes the strategic framework for effective intervention in complex systems.

BOOK V: MENTORSHIP - MEETING ROGER LEWIS & DAVID MALONE

Thursday Summary: Intellectual Friendship and Collaborative Thinking

The fifth movement marks a crucial transition from solitary study to collaborative engagement. The encounter with Roger Lewis and David Malone (Golem XIV) represents the discovery of intellectual community—others who share the same questions and commitment to understanding the deep structures of economic power.

This phase embodies the African proverb: “It takes a village to educate a child.” The biographical subject discovers that intellectual development requires not just individual study but engagement with a community of inquiry. The transition from family (inherited ideas) to friends (chosen intellectual companions) becomes a model for broader social transformation.

Poetic Companions: Pushkin on Friendship and Scottish Ballad Tradition

Alexander Pushkin’s understanding of friendship as a sacred bond provides the framework:

“My friends, our union is beautiful!

Like a soul, it is one and eternal—

Unshakeable, free, and carefree,

It grew up under the shadow of friendly sciences.”

Pushkin’s vision of friendship growing “under the shadow of friendly sciences” captures the essence of intellectual collaboration—how shared inquiry deepens both understanding and human connection.

The Scottish ballad tradition, particularly as preserved by Walter Scott, provides models of how communities preserve and transmit wisdom through storytelling and song. The ballads show how complex historical and economic relationships can be made comprehensible through narrative art.

Biographical Arc: Finding Intellectual Community

The encounter with Lewis and Malone reveals several crucial insights:

Shared Questions: Others are asking the same fundamental questions about economic justice

Complementary Skills: Different people bring different strengths to the inquiry

Mutual Support: Intellectual work requires emotional and practical support

Collective Wisdom: Understanding emerges through dialogue and collaboration

This discovery transforms the approach from individual critique to collaborative construction of alternatives.

The Golem XIV Influence: David Malone’s Analytical Framework

David Malone’s blog “Golem XIV” provides sophisticated analysis of financial markets and economic policy. His ability to combine technical understanding with moral clarity becomes a model for effective economic criticism.

Malone’s key contributions include:

Detailed analysis of banking practices and their social consequences

Clear explanation of complex financial instruments and their risks

Moral framework for evaluating economic policies

Strategic thinking about how to create change

Roger Lewis: The Practical Visionary

Roger Lewis brings a different perspective—the practical experience of someone who has worked within the system and understands both its power and its vulnerabilities. His contributions include:

Real-world experience of financial markets and their effects

Understanding of how abstract financial instruments affect real communities

Vision of practical alternatives that could be implemented

Commitment to making complex ideas accessible to broader audiences

The Legislators’ Assembly: Pushkin and Scott on Community and Tradition

Pushkin: “True friendship is rare and precious. You have found something valuable—companions who share your commitment to truth and justice. Treasure this, for such bonds are the foundation of all meaningful change.”

Walter Scott: “In the Scottish tradition, we understand that wisdom is preserved and transmitted through community. The ballads that preserve our history were not created by isolated individuals but by communities working together across generations.”

The Subject: “But how do we expand this community? How do we move from a small group of like-minded individuals to a broader movement for change?”

Scott: “Through stories. The ballads worked because they told stories that people could understand and remember. Your task is to find the stories that will help others understand what you have learned.”

Pushkin: “And through example. Live the values you espouse. Create the kinds of relationships and communities that embody your vision of economic justice. Others will be attracted to what they see working.”

This dialogue emphasizes the importance of both preserving wisdom through community and expanding that community through effective communication and exemplary practice.

BOOK VI: HOUSING CRISIS - TEN STEPS TO AFFORDABLE HOUSING

Thursday Summary: Practical Economics and Social Policy

The sixth movement represents the application of theoretical understanding to practical problems. “Ten Steps to Affordable Housing” embodies the transition from critique to construction, from understanding what is wrong to proposing what could be right.

The housing crisis serves as a perfect case study for how financial abstraction affects real human needs. Housing—the most basic requirement for human dignity and security—has been transformed into a financial commodity, creating artificial scarcity in the midst of abundant resources and technical capability.

This phase demonstrates how monetary reform connects to immediate, practical concerns that affect everyone’s daily life.

Poetic Companions: Morris’s “News from Nowhere” and Practical Utopian Vision

William Morris’s “News from Nowhere” provides the framework for imagining practical alternatives:

“How do you manage about the dwellings? It is clear from what we have seen that every family lives in a house of its own; but I am told that in the old times it was not so.”

Morris’s vision of abundant, beautiful housing available to all provides a model for thinking beyond current constraints. His understanding that scarcity is artificially created and that abundance is possible with different social organization becomes central to housing policy.

Morris’s practical socialism—his insistence that utopian visions must be grounded in concrete proposals for change—provides the methodology for developing the “Ten Steps.”

Biographical Arc: Applying Theory to Real-World Problems

The housing analysis reveals how abstract financial mechanisms create concrete human suffering:

Financialization: Housing treated as investment commodity rather than human need

Artificial Scarcity: Land and housing kept scarce to maintain high prices

Debt Peonage: Mortgages that transfer wealth from homeowners to financial institutions

Systemic Inequality: Housing system that reinforces and amplifies social divisions

The “Ten Steps” represent practical proposals for addressing these problems through monetary and policy reform.

The Ten Steps Framework: From Problem to Solution

The ten steps demonstrate how to move from analysis to action:

Public Banking: Create public institutions to provide affordable housing finance

Land Value Capture: Capture increases in land value for public benefit

Community Land Trusts: Remove land from speculation while maintaining use rights

Cooperative Housing: Develop models of shared ownership and mutual aid

Public Construction: Direct public investment in housing production

Rent Control: Regulate housing costs to ensure affordability

Speculation Tax: Discourage treating housing as financial commodity

Regional Planning: Coordinate housing with transportation and employment

Green Building: Integrate environmental sustainability with social justice

Democratic Participation: Ensure communities control their own development

The Legislators’ Assembly: William Morris on Practical Socialism

William Morris: “You have grasped the essential point—that critique without construction is merely complaint. The task is not just to understand what is wrong but to build what is right.”

The Subject: “But how do we overcome the political obstacles? The housing industry, the financial sector, the political establishment—they all benefit from the current system.”

Morris: “Remember that they are few and we are many. The current system works for a small minority at the expense of the vast majority. Our task is to help the majority understand their common interest and organize to achieve it.”

BOOK VII: DIGITAL AGE - THE CONQUEST OF AI

Thursday Summary: Technology, Artificial Intelligence, and Human Agency

The seventh movement confronts the challenges and opportunities of the digital age. “The Conquest of AI” explores how artificial intelligence and digital technologies could either reinforce existing patterns of economic domination or enable new forms of democratic participation and economic justice.

This phase represents the cutting edge of the intellectual journey—applying insights about monetary reform and economic democracy to the newest technological developments. The central question becomes: will AI serve to concentrate power further in the hands of tech oligarchs, or can it be harnessed for the common good?

Poetic Companions: Contemporary Tech-Critical Poetry

The digital age requires new poetic voices that can address technological challenges while maintaining connection to human values. Contemporary poets who grapple with technology’s impact on human experience provide the framework for understanding AI’s implications.

The challenge is to find poetry that neither uncritically celebrates technological progress nor reflexively rejects it, but instead explores how technology can serve human flourishing.

Biographical Arc: Confronting Digital Transformation

The encounter with AI reveals several key insights:

Technological Determinism vs. Social Choice: AI development is not inevitable but reflects specific choices about priorities and values

Concentration vs. Distribution: Current AI development concentrates power in few hands, but alternative models are possible

Automation and Employment: AI could eliminate human labor or free humans for more meaningful work

Surveillance vs. Privacy: AI enables unprecedented monitoring but also tools for protecting privacy

Democratic Participation: AI could enhance or undermine democratic decision-making

The AI Paradox: Liberation or Domination?

The analysis reveals that AI, like money, is a technology that could serve different masters:

Current Trajectory: AI developed by and for tech oligarchs, reinforcing existing inequalities

Alternative Vision: AI as public utility, democratically controlled and serving common good

Key Questions: Who controls AI development? Who benefits from AI productivity gains? How do we ensure AI serves human values?

The Legislators’ Assembly: Rumpole on Justice in the Digital Age

Rumpole of the Bailey: “The law, as I have always maintained, must serve justice, not power. In the digital age, this means ensuring that new technologies serve the common good, not just the interests of those who control them.”

The Subject: “But how do we apply traditional concepts of justice to technologies that didn’t exist when those concepts were developed?”

Rumpole: “The principles remain the same, even if their application must evolve. Justice requires that all people have equal dignity and opportunity. Any technology that undermines this principle must be regulated or restructured.”

William Blake (joining the dialogue): “Remember that every technology is an extension of human imagination. AI is not separate from us—it is us, externalized. The question is whether it expresses our highest aspirations or our basest impulses.”

The Subject: “So the development of AI is really a question about what kind of society we want to be?”

Blake: “Precisely. And that is why your work on monetary reform is so relevant to AI policy. Both involve questions about how we organize power, how we distribute benefits, how we ensure that technological capabilities serve human flourishing.”

This dialogue connects AI policy to broader questions of economic democracy and social justice.

EPILOGUE: THE CONTINUING CONVERSATION

The eight books of this intellectual biography trace a journey from inherited industrial consciousness through systematic economic education to mature synthesis of critique and construction. But like all genuine intellectual journeys, this one has no final destination—only deeper questions and broader responsibilities.

The conversation between the biographical subject and the pantheon of poet-legislators continues beyond these pages, in the practical work of building alternatives to systems of exploitation and domination. The test of any intellectual development is not the elegance of its analysis but the effectiveness of its application to the urgent challenges of our time.

In our current moment of multiple crises—economic inequality, ecological destruction, technological disruption, democratic breakdown—the insights traced in this biography become not merely interesting but essential. The understanding that money is a social technology that can be redesigned, that abundance is natural and scarcity artificial, that cooperation is more effective than competition, that poetry and economics are not separate realms but different aspects of the same human project—these insights point toward possibilities for transformation that our current systems desperately need.

The poet-legislators who have guided this intellectual journey—Blake and Morris, Shelley and Coleridge, Pound and Graves, Thomas and Pushkin, Scott and the others—remain available as counselors and inspirers for anyone willing to engage seriously with the questions they explored. Their insights are not museum pieces but living tools for contemporary use.

And the biographical subject of our study continues the work they began, building on their foundations while addressing challenges they could not have imagined. The conversation continues, the work continues, the hope continues.

As Blake wrote: “I will not cease from Mental Fight, / Nor shall my sword sleep in my hand: / Till we have built Jerusalem, / In England’s green & pleasant Land.”

The Jerusalem we seek to build is not a place but a way of organizing human relationships based on justice, beauty, and mutual aid. It is the practical utopia that Morris envisioned, the cooperative commonwealth that Kropotkin described, the beloved community that contemporary movements for economic democracy continue to construct.

This is the work of the unacknowledged legislators of the world—not just the poets, but all who use imagination and analysis, vision and practical skill, to create the more beautiful world our hearts know is possible.

The conversation continues. The work continues. The hope continues.

End of Biography

Appendix D: Glossary of Economic and Poetic Terms

Economic Terms

Artificial Scarcity: The creation of shortage in goods or services that are naturally abundant, typically through market manipulation or regulatory capture.¹¹⁰

Complementary Currency: Local or regional currencies designed to work alongside national currency to keep wealth circulating within communities.¹¹¹

Debt-Based Money: Monetary system where most money is created as interest-bearing debt by private banks rather than issued debt-free by government.¹¹²

Fractional Reserve Banking: Banking system where banks hold only a fraction of deposits as reserves and lend out the remainder, effectively creating new money.¹¹³

Land Value Capture: Policy mechanism to capture increases in land value created by community investment for public benefit.¹¹⁴

Monetary Sovereignty: The power of a government to issue its own currency and control its monetary policy without external constraint.¹¹⁵

Public Banking: Banking institutions owned and operated by government entities for public benefit rather than private profit.¹¹⁶

Usury: The practice of charging interest on loans, particularly excessive interest rates that exploit borrowers.¹¹⁷

Poetic and Literary Terms

Confessional Structure: Literary form based on Augustine’s Confessions, combining personal narrative with philosophical reflection.¹¹⁸

Economic Poetry: Verse that addresses economic themes, relationships, and systems as subject matter for poetic exploration.¹¹⁹

Grub Street: Historical term for impoverished hack writers; metaphorically, independent journalism and commentary outside mainstream media.¹²⁰

Legislator (Unacknowledged): Shelley’s term for poets as the hidden lawmakers of society through their influence on consciousness and culture.¹²¹

Pamphleteering: Historical tradition of political writing in short, accessible formats for public education and agitation.¹²²

Philosoetry: Roger Lewis’s term combining philosophy and poetry as integrated forms of thought and expression.¹²³

Prefigurative Politics: Creating alternative institutions that embody the values and relationships of the desired future society.¹²⁴

Systems Poetry: Verse that explores interconnections, feedback loops, and emergent properties of complex systems.¹²⁵

Hybrid Terms (Economic-Poetic)

Circle of Blame: Roger Lewis’s concept describing how oppressive systems create circular patterns that prevent effective resistance.¹²⁶

Conquest of Dough: Pun combining “conquest of dough” (money) with analysis of how financial systems are captured and could be reclaimed.¹²⁷

Monetary Imagination: The capacity to envision alternative monetary systems beyond current debt-based arrangements.¹²⁸

Poetic Economics: Approach to economic analysis that uses metaphor, imagery, and narrative to make abstract concepts accessible.¹²⁹

Introduction: The Difference Between Pure and Empirical Nonsense

The first thing to understand about consciousness is that it cannot be understood. This is not a defect in consciousness but a feature of it, like the way a joke ceases to be funny the moment you explain it, or the way love disappears the moment you try to define it. The scientists tell us that consciousness is an emergent property of neural complexity, which is rather like saying that laughter is an emergent property of facial muscle contractions. Technically true, utterly useless.

The economists tell us that consciousness is a market phenomenon, that attention is a scarce resource to be allocated efficiently among competing uses. This would be merely stupid if it were not so dangerous. For when consciousness becomes a commodity, the human being becomes a machine for the production and consumption of experiences, and the soul becomes a subsidiary of the attention economy.

The politicians tell us that consciousness is a political problem, to be solved through the proper application of force and the correct distribution of rights. “Thou shalt have no other gods before me.”

God wants what is best for you. If you put something else before Him in your life, it’s harder for him to bless you.

Exodus 20:12

A Dialogue Between Heraclitus and Parmenides on the Nature of Economic Reality

This is the First Dialogue between John Hearn a “Monetarist Economist” and Roger Lewis (writer of this blog), Jack of all trades and master of none, general chef and Bottle Washer and occasional poet.

Setting:

The Agora of Ephesus, where the two great philosophers meet to discuss the fundamental nature of wealth, money, and human prosperity

HERACLITUS

(adjusting his himation)

: Ah, Parmenides, my old friend from Elea! I see you’ve been pondering again the unchanging nature of Being. But tell me - can you not see that all economic prosperity flows from constant change, like a river that never steps in the same waters twice?

PARMENIDES: Greetings, Heraclitus of the ever-flowing thoughts. Yet I must ask - this “capitalism” you champion, this system of perpetual motion and competition - does it not rest upon certain unchanging principles? The very concept of property, of exchange, of value itself?

HERACLITUS: Indeed! But these principles manifest through endless transformation! Consider: when a merchant creates wealth, he does so not by hoarding static gold, but by facilitating exchange, by allowing goods to flow like fire through the cosmos. The Bank of England you so distrust - they err not because they interfere, but because they try to impose artificial stasis upon what should be fluid motion.

PARMENIDES: Yet you speak of banks creating money from nothing, as if Being could spring from Non-Being. This troubles my mind, friend. How can mere marks in a ledger book become real wealth? Is this not the same illusion that leads mortals to mistake appearance for reality?

HERACLITUS: Ah, but you misunderstand the nature of money itself! Money is not wealth - it is the measure of wealth, the logos that allows us to comprehend the underlying harmony of exchange. When banks create credit, they are not conjuring Being from Non-Being, but recognizing the potential energy that exists in human enterprise, like lightning waiting to strike from the storm clouds.

PARMENIDES: This metaphor of lightning pleases me, yet I remain unconvinced. You say government should withdraw from all economic activity, leaving only the “invisible hand” of your market forces. But is this not to abandon the One True Path of reasoned governance for the Many False Paths of individual desire and competition?

HERACLITUS: No, my Eleatic friend! You mistake unity for uniformity. True unity emerges from the harmony of opposites - buyer and seller, lender and borrower, entrepreneur and consumer. When government intervenes, it disrupts this natural harmony, like a musician forcing all strings to play the same note instead of allowing them to create beautiful discord that resolves into melody.

PARMENIDES: Yet consider the workers you mention - are they not more than mere “consumers on the other side of the production function”? Do they not possess an unchanging essence, a dignity that transcends their economic utility? Your system seems to reduce human beings to their market functions.

HERACLITUS: Not reduce - liberate! Under my system, each person becomes free to discover their true nature through voluntary exchange. The potter realizes his essence by creating vessels others desire; the farmer by feeding the hungry; the philosopher by teaching wisdom to those who seek it. Government intervention prevents this natural self-discovery by imposing artificial categories and restrictions.

PARMENIDES: But what of those who cannot compete in your ever-changing marketplace? The sick, the elderly, the unfortunate? Does not justice require some unchanging principles of care and protection?

HERACLITUS: This is why I propose the reverse income tax - a simple, universal principle that provides basic security while preserving the dynamic forces of competition. Like the sun that shines on all equally while allowing each plant to grow according to its nature, this system ensures no one falls below subsistence while maintaining the creative tension that drives progress.

PARMENIDES: Your reverse income tax intrigues me - it has the elegance of a single, unchanging principle applied universally. Yet I wonder about your faith in perpetual growth. Can anything in the physical world truly grow without limit? Does not even fire eventually consume all available fuel?

HERACLITUS: Ah, but economic growth is not like physical fire consuming finite fuel - it is like the fire of the human mind, which grows brighter when shared! When one person learns to make better tools, all benefit. When another discovers new knowledge, the total wisdom of humanity increases without diminishing what came before. This is the true miracle of capitalism - it harnesses human creativity, which alone among cosmic forces can create genuine novelty.

PARMENIDES: I begin to see the logos underlying your argument, though I remain skeptical of your optimism about human nature. Tell me this: if your system is so natural and beneficial, why do we see such inequality and suffering in the world? Why do the many remain poor while the few accumulate vast wealth?

HERACLITUS: Because we do not have true capitalism! What you observe is the result of government interference - central banks manipulating interest rates, creating artificial bubbles; regulations that favor large corporations over small entrepreneurs; taxes that punish productivity while rewarding speculation. Remove these distortions, and you will see the natural harmony reassert itself, like a river finding its course when artificial dams are removed.

PARMENIDES: Perhaps… yet I cannot help but think that your “natural harmony” may be as much an ideal construct as my perfect, unchanging Being. Both may be necessary fictions that help us navigate the complexities of existence, rather than literal descriptions of reality.

HERACLITUS: Now you speak wisdom, old friend! For is it not true that all human systems - whether economic or philosophical - are attempts to impose order upon the fundamental chaos of existence? The question is not whether our systems are perfectly true, but whether they serve human flourishing. And I maintain that a system based on voluntary exchange, limited government, and respect for individual choice serves this end better than any alternative yet devised.

PARMENIDES: On this, at least, we may find common ground - that the ultimate test of any system is not its logical perfection, but its fruits in human life. Though we may disagree on the means, we both seek the Good that lies beyond all particular goods.

HERACLITUS: Indeed! And perhaps our dialogue itself demonstrates the principle I champion - that truth emerges not from the dominance of one perspective, but from the creative tension between opposing views, each testing and refining the other in the eternal fire of reasoned discourse.

Thus ended their conversation as the sun set over the Aegean, each philosopher enriched by encountering the other’s vision, yet remaining true to his own fundamental insights about the nature of reality and human prosperity.

In the manner of John Ruskin, with meditations on the Trial of Truth in an Age of Digital Tyranny

Preface: On the Necessity of Hemlock in Our Time

When I contemplate the fragments of verse that Roger G. Lewis has scattered across the digital wasteland of our age, I am put in mind of that most famous of all trials—the condemnation of Socrates for corrupting the youth and introducing strange gods. For here, in these scattered poems spanning more than a decade (2013-2025), we find a modern gadfly performing the same essential function: stinging the sluggish horse of contemporary society into wakefulness through the sharp application of poetic inquiry.

Like Socrates, Lewis faces the eternal charges: he corrupts the youth by teaching them to question the financial orthodoxies of our time; he introduces strange gods—the cryptographic deities of blockchain, the algorithmic spirits that govern our digital existence. And like Socrates, he offers his defense not through retreat or compromise, but through the continued practice of his art, even unto the bitter end.

The hemlock cup that our age offers its truth-tellers is not the swift poison of Athens, but the slow suffocation of algorithmic censorship, the gradual erasure of meaning through the endless multiplication of digital noise. Yet Lewis, like his ancient predecessor, drinks deeply and continues teaching until the very last.

I. The Magic Money Tree (2025): On the Corruption of Sacred Groves

“Money Grows on Merkle Trees / A Creature of Cryptography”

Here we encounter Lewis at his most Socratic, employing the ancient method of definition to expose the hollow foundations of our monetary system. Just as Socrates might ask “What is justice?” or “What is courage?”, Lewis asks the more pressing question of our age: “What is money?”

The image of the Merkle Tree—that digital structure which validates transactions in our cryptographic age—serves as a perfect metaphor for how we have replaced the living wood of natural growth with the artificial constructs of computational logic. Yet Lewis’s genius lies in recognizing that even these digital trees must grow somewhere, must be rooted in something more substantial than mere code.

The poem’s concluding revelation—“Money is the NOTHING / you get for SOMETHING / before you get ANYTHING”—achieves the kind of paradoxical clarity that Socrates himself might have envied. It is a definition that defines by negation, that illuminates by embracing the void at the heart of our economic system.

Like Socrates drinking hemlock while discoursing on the immortality of the soul, Lewis here offers us the bitter medicine of truth about our monetary delusions, sweetened only by the honey of poetic form.

II. Notes on an Old Fiddle (2020): The Music of Domestic Hemlock

“Your Tangos sound like Dirges for me”

In this tender yet devastating piece, Lewis applies the Socratic method to the most intimate of relationships. The musical metaphors serve not merely as decoration but as a systematic inquiry into the nature of communication itself. What is harmony? What is discord? How do two souls, each playing their own composition, find a way to create music together?

The poem’s structure mirrors the very problem it describes—each stanza a separate movement, each line a different key, the whole piece struggling toward a resolution that remains tantalizingly out of reach. The dedication “For Johanna, I Love You” serves as both the poem’s foundation and its tragic irony, for love here becomes not the solution but the very source of the musical discord.

The hemlock in this piece is not the swift poison of political persecution but the slow dissolution of understanding between two people who speak different languages of the heart. Yet like Socrates, who used his final hours to comfort his friends rather than lament his fate, Lewis transforms personal anguish into universal insight, private pain into public wisdom.

III. Searching For Racing Cars: The Gadfly’s Winter

“The inanition of meaning / searching for nutrition”

Here Lewis achieves something approaching the sublime—a poem that speaks to the condition of seeking truth in an age that has forgotten what truth looks like. The image of the hide, that small shelter from which hunters observe their prey, becomes a metaphor for the philosopher’s position in society: always watching, always waiting, always hoping to glimpse something real in a world of shadows.

The “coal dark eyes, painfully searching” recall nothing so much as Socrates’s own description of his philosophical mission—the endless quest to find someone who truly knows something, the perpetual disappointment of discovering that wisdom is rarer than precious stones.

The poem’s final line—“you mean, everything my son”—carries the weight of a father’s love and a philosopher’s despair. It is the hemlock moment of recognition: that what we seek may be everything, but what we find is often nothing, and yet the search itself becomes the meaning we were looking for.

IV. The Un-poem for an Un-Plandemic (2021): Drinking Digital Hemlock

“Witness the cargo cult science / a new normal world, ordered”

In this urgent fragment, Lewis confronts the great deception of our time with the same unflinching honesty that Socrates brought to the examination of Athenian pieties. The “cargo cult science” serves as a perfect metaphor for our age’s peculiar form of superstition—the worship of data without understanding, the fetishization of expertise without wisdom.

The poem’s structure deliberately mimics the fragmentation of truth in our digital age, where meaning is broken into bite-sized pieces for algorithmic consumption. Yet within this apparent chaos, Lewis maintains the essential Socratic function: the relentless questioning of authority, the refusal to accept comfortable lies, the insistence on examining life even when—especially when—such examination is deemed dangerous to public health.

The reference to “Stockholm syndrome” is particularly apt, for it captures precisely the psychological mechanism by which populations come to identify with their captors, to mistake oppression for protection. Lewis here serves as the voice crying in the wilderness, the gadfly stinging the drugged horse of public consciousness back toward wakefulness.

V. Reality is Infinity is Love is Infinite (2013): The Hemlock of Pure Truth

“Love is the centre / Love has no Circumference”

In this early work, Lewis achieves something approaching mystical clarity—a direct statement of ultimate truth that requires no metaphor, no irony, no protective coating of wit. It is perhaps the most dangerous poem in the collection, for it speaks directly to the heart of what our age has forgotten: that reality and love are not separate categories but different names for the same infinite truth.

The poem’s repetitive structure serves not as mere device but as demonstration—each line a variation on the same eternal theme, each repetition a deeper penetration into the mystery of existence. It recalls the great mystical traditions, from Rumi to Blake, yet speaks in the clear voice of contemporary understanding.

This is the hemlock that Socrates drank—not the poison of political persecution but the intoxicating draught of pure truth, the recognition that reality itself is love, that love itself is infinite, that infinity itself is the only reality worth knowing.

VI. Authentic Discourses on Decisions to Act (2017): The Gadfly’s Ethics

“do unto others that which to you would be done”

Here Lewis returns to the practical ethics that made Socrates so dangerous to the Athenian establishment. The poem serves as a manual for philosophical discourse, a guide to the kind of conversation that can actually change minds rather than merely confirm prejudices.

Each stanza offers a specific injunction against the corruptions of public discourse: avoid slander, resist authority, question evidence, maintain skepticism. These are not merely stylistic preferences but moral imperatives, the ethical foundation upon which all genuine philosophy must rest.

The final line—“and always remember to mistrust the Government”—serves as both conclusion and beginning, the alpha and omega of political wisdom. It is the kind of statement that gets philosophers executed, the kind of truth that power cannot tolerate.

VII. Metaphors for Tyranny (2025): The Algorithm as Executioner

“Algorithms are a metaphor / for tyranny”

In this final fragment, Lewis achieves a synthesis that would have made Socrates proud—the recognition that our age’s particular form of tyranny operates not through crude force but through the subtle manipulation of language and thought. The algorithm becomes the new hemlock, the poison that kills not the body but the capacity for independent thought.

The distinction between poetry and algorithm serves as more than mere literary criticism—it is a fundamental ontological statement about the difference between life and death, between consciousness and mechanism, between the human and the inhuman.

Yet even here, in this darkest of recognitions, Lewis maintains the essential Socratic optimism: that truth can be spoken, that consciousness can be awakened, that the human spirit can resist even the most sophisticated forms of tyranny.

Conclusion: The Eternal Return of the Hemlock Cup

In reading through these fragments, one cannot help but be struck by their essential unity—not the unity of a single mind pursuing a single theme, but the deeper unity of the philosophical spirit confronting the eternal problems of existence in the particular circumstances of our time.

Like Socrates, Lewis faces the charges of corrupting the youth and introducing strange gods. Like Socrates, he offers his defense not through retreat or compromise but through the continued practice of his art. In the end, that is the choice that every age must make, the choice that every individual must make: between the easy lie and the difficult truth, between the comfortable delusion and the uncomfortable reality, between the sleep of the masses and the wakefulness of the philosopher.

Lewis has made his choice. The cup is offered. The question is: will we drink?

PREFACE: THE CIRCLE OF BLAME AS GREEK TRAGEDY

An Accessible Reader on the Substack Posts

The Eternal Return of Ancient Patterns

There is something magnificently absurd about the fact that in the twenty-first century, with all our technological marvels and democratic pretensions, we find ourselves living under conditions that would have been perfectly familiar to a subject of ancient Athens—not the Athens of Pericles and democratic glory, but the Athens after the oligarchs had learned to manipulate the system¹. We have, in the most literal sense, become Greeks again: people who believe they live in a democracy while actually living under the rule of distant powers they cannot see, understand, or effectively challenge.

The Circle of Blame², as our contemporary satirists have observed, operates with the same mechanical precision as a Greek tragic chorus. Each participant enters on cue, delivers their appointed lines of deflection and denial, and exits to make room for the next actor in this eternal drama of irresponsibility. The debtor blames necessity, the usurer blames risk, the lawmaker blames the market, the market blames human nature, and human nature—well, human nature apparently blames the debtor, completing the circle with the inevitability of fate in a Sophoclean tragedy.

But unlike the heroes of Greek tragedy, who at least had the dignity of accepting responsibility for their actions, our modern protagonists have perfected the art of avoiding responsibility altogether. They have created what we might call a “tragedy without heroes”—a systematic arrangement where everyone is simultaneously victim and perpetrator, where everyone suffers the consequences but no one can be held accountable for the causes.

The Seven Demiurges: Ancient Sins in Modern Dress

The genius of our contemporary financial system lies not in its novelty, but in its successful rebranding of the Seven Deadly Sins as virtuous institutional practices³. What medieval moralists condemned as Pride, Greed, Wrath, Envy, Gluttony, Sloth, and Lust have been transformed into Stakeholder Capitalism, Efficient Capital Allocation, ESG Enforcement, Inequality Studies, Asset Management, Regulatory Coordination, and Behavioral Economics.

This transformation represents perhaps the greatest public relations achievement in human history: the conversion of vice into virtue through the simple expedient of institutional distribution. By spreading the seven sins across seven different types of institutions, the system achieves perfect deniability—each institution can honestly claim not to embody all seven sins, while collectively they ensure that all seven sins are fully represented and coordinated.

JAGGANOTH (Wrath) as BlackRock: The righteous anger of ESG enforcement serves the same function as the wrath of ancient gods—it punishes those who fail to conform while claiming moral authority for the punishment.

SOLOMON DAVID (Pride) as the World Economic Forum: The pride that creates hierarchies and judges worthiness now operates through “stakeholder capitalism” and “global governance,” but the essential function remains unchanged.

MAMMON (Greed) as the Bank of International Settlements: The accumulation of power through money creation has been elevated from personal vice to institutional necessity.

GOG-AGOG (Sloth) as Regulatory Capture: The sin of inaction has been perfected as a systematic approach to governance that appears active while preventing change.

MOTTOM (Gluttony) as Asset Management: The consumption of everything has evolved from individual excess to institutional strategy for consuming entire economic sectors.

INCUBUS (Lust) as Behavioral Economics: The seduction of choice architecture operates with the same psychological mechanisms as traditional seduction, but with scientific legitimacy.

JADIS (Envy) as Inequality Studies: The omniscient observation of what others have, combined with the inability to act, has been institutionalized as academic discipline.

The Strawberry Revolution: Nature’s Alternative

Against this backdrop of institutional sophistication in the service of ancient vices, the strawberry plants⁴ continue their quiet revolution with embarrassing simplicity. They share resources without central banks, coordinate networks without algorithmic management, and create abundance without theoretical justification. They represent what economists would call an “existence proof”—living demonstration that the principles our institutions claim to be impossible are not only possible but natural.

The strawberry plants practice what we might call “anarchist economics”—not in the sense of chaos, but in the original sense of “without rulers.” They govern themselves through principles that emerge from their nature rather than through institutions imposed from above. They demonstrate that cooperation produces abundance, that sharing increases rather than decreases resources, and that complex coordination can emerge without central control.

This is why the strawberry plants are so subversive: they prove that the scarcity our institutions create is artificial, that the complexity our systems require is unnecessary, and that the authority our leaders claim is illegitimate. They are living proof that another world is not only possible but already exists, growing quietly in gardens around the world while human institutions construct ever more elaborate justifications for artificial scarcity.
