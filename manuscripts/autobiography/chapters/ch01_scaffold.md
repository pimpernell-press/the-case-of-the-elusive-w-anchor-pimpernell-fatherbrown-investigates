### Register Guidance for the Witness Voice

The model is the 245-word foreword from the Poetic Legislator. The
register is:

- First person, past tense, memory register
- Short sentences for specific memories: "I remember my sister Suzanne typing early drafts."
- Longer sentences when the memory opens out into reflection
- Concrete details: the glass of Worthington's, the television, the dining table
- No rhetoric, no analysis, no economics — that comes later
- The warmth of the remembered household, without sentimentality
- The specificity of place: Talbot Green, Oxfordshire, the Valleys
- The presence of the grandparents as physical people, not historical figures

Also available as register models:
- "99 Flakes for Sale" (Poem 50) — childhood memory, the dropped ice cream, the hand
- "Searching for Racing Cars" (Poem 51) — "coal dark eyes, painfully searching"
- DoughForge ch008 — "I was transported back immediately to long evenings at the dining table"

The Witness voice is not the prophetic voice. It is not the Conquest of
Dough voice. It is not the blog voice. It is the voice of a man at a
kitchen table, remembering.

## Section 1: The Deep Time

*Surveyor voice. ~250 words. The geological and historical context.*

The South Wales coalfield extends approximately 1,000 square miles, from Pontypool in the east to St Bride's Bay in the west. The coal measures were laid down in the Carboniferous period, roughly 310 million years ago, when the landmass that would become Wales sat near the equator and the forests that would become coal grew in swampy lowlands under a tropical sun. By the time men descended to extract it, the coal had been waiting longer than any civilisation.

The mining communities that formed above the seams — Tonyrefail, Tonypandy, Treorchy, Talbot Green — were built on the principle that individual survival depended on collective solidarity. Cooperative societies, mutual aid associations, chapel libraries, the Mechanics' Institute tradition, poetry recitation — what the Welsh call *cythraul canu*, the music devil, the compulsion to sing. The culture was not a byproduct of the mining. The mining was the economic base; the culture was the purpose.

Foster Lewis was born in 1902 in this world. He started school in 1905 wearing petticoats, because boys in Tonyrefail were not put into trousers until the age of five. He remembered it clearly in his eighties because of the toffee — the teacher had slipped one into his clasped hands during prayers, and he had thought, even then, that she had done a wicked thing. He still thought so. The memory had not softened in eighty years.

His grandson Roger was born approximately sixty years later — not in the Valleys, but in a household that carried the Valleys with it. The childhood was in Oxfordshire, in a village called Barford St Michael. But the grandparents' house was in South Wales, and the ration still arrived from the brewery, and the dining table was where the two worlds met.

## Section 3: The Two Grandfathers

*Surveyor voice. ~250 words. The factual record of both grandfathers.*

Roger Lewis's maternal grandfather was Llewellyn Harrison. The foreword names him alongside Foster Lewis as part of the same generation — men who "having neutralised the worst excesses of one Owning Class" would be "horrified that the country is now in the grip of an Owning Class much worse than their forebears."

Two grandfathers. Two lineages. Both from the communities built above the coal measures. Foster Lewis left a memoir — *The Miner's Tale*, first printed for the family in 1985, typed by his granddaughter Suzanne, sent back and forth between Oxfordshire and Talbot Green. His wife Doris read to him throughout, because his eyesight was failing. Dr Hwyel Francis at the South Wales Miners' Library helped with the photographs. The Welsh Industrial Museum helped with the copying. It was a family production, made with the same cooperative instinct that had sustained the mining communities through everything the twentieth century had thrown at them.

Llewellyn Harrison did not leave a memoir. His record is carried in the family's memory alone. The asymmetry matters: one grandfather's witness survives in print; the other's survives only in the people who remember him.

## Section 5: The Ration

*Witness voice. ~400 words. The brewery beer as fact, not metaphor.*

> **[WITNESS]**
>
> **Author prompt:** "The ration still sent every month from the brewery." In DoughForge ch008 you wrote: "The ration still arriving. From a world that had already ended. That detail has not left me."
>
> This is the central image of the chapter. The ration is:
> - A literal fact: Worthington's pale ale, delivered monthly to the household
> - A survivor from a previous economic arrangement (the brewery ration — was this a retirement benefit? A union entitlement? A tradition?)
> - A thing that continued after the world that created it had ended
> - The origin of the metaphor that names DoughForge ch008
>
> Questions:
> - Where did the ration come from? Was it a brewery pension benefit? A union agreement? A custom?
> - How did it arrive — by post? By delivery? Did someone bring it?
> - How long did it continue? Was it still arriving when you were a child in the late 1960s/early 1970s?
> - Was it always Worthington's? Was there a choice, or was Worthington's what the brewery sent?
> - Did Grampy drink anything else, or was the ration THE drink?
> - When did the ration stop? Did it stop when Grampy died? When the brewery closed? When the agreement was cancelled?
> - Did you understand, as a child, that the ration was unusual — that not everyone's grandfather had beer delivered monthly from a previous life?
> - When did you first realise that "the ration still arriving" was a metaphor for something larger?
>
> **Register:** Begin with the physical fact. The bottles (or cans). The delivery. The routine. Then let the reflection arrive naturally — "that detail has not left me." Don't force the metaphor. The metaphor will arrive because the fact is already larger than itself.
>
> **Target:** ~400 words.

## Section 6: The Baards

*Surveyor voice. ~200 words. The Welsh bardic tradition. Janos Arany.*

The foreword ends with a quotation:

> *'A thousand lie beneath the sky,*
> *They rot beneath the sun,*
> *And we who live shall not forgive*
> *This deed your hand hath done!'*
>
> *Baards of Wales. Janos Arany*

Janos Arany's "The Bards of Wales" (1857) tells of Edward I ordering the massacre of Welsh bards after the conquest of Wales in 1282, to prevent them singing songs of resistance. The poem was written in Hungarian, about Wales, as a veiled protest against Austrian Habsburg rule — the oppressed using another oppression's story to speak their own. The bards were burned for speaking truth against the tyrant. The poem survives because the truth it spoke was not only Welsh.

Roger Lewis places this quotation at the end of his grandfather's foreword. The bards and the miners share a tradition: the compulsion to speak, the punishment for speaking, the survival of the speech. *Cythraul canu* — the music devil — is not a metaphor in this family. It is, as the grandson would later write, a hereditary condition.

## Key Correction from Corpus Mine

**The childhood was in Oxfordshire.** The 01_outline.md assumed South Wales;
the Barford St Michael transcript reveals Roger grew up in an English village,
attending Warriner school, playing cricket in Bloxom. The grandparents' house
in South Wales was the visited world, not the everyday world. This changes
Chapter 1's framing: the dining table is the place where two Englands meet —
the Oxfordshire village and the Welsh valleys — and the child travels between them.

Chapter 2 (The Moleskin Trousers) must now be reframed as the Oxfordshire
childhood + West Germany + military boarding school, not the Valleys.
