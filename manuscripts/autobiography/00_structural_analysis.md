## 2. Voice Register Analysis

**The biography (Books I–VIII) is written in THIRD PERSON — "the biographical subject," "our subject," "the consciousness being formed."** This is not autobiography. It is a critical biography written *about* Roger Lewis, using the conventions of literary biography: the distanced analytical voice, "our study," "the intellectual journey we are tracing."

The voice shifts across the three parts:

| Part | Register | Person | Character |
|------|----------|--------|-----------|
| Biography (Books I–VIII) | Critical/analytical | Third person | "The biographical subject encounters..." "Our study inherits..." Literary biography convention. |
| Companion Edition + Apparatus | Editorial/reference | Third person | Cross-references, glossary, discussion questions. Scholarly apparatus. |
| Poems and Essays | Prophetic/personal | First person + dramatic | Roger's own voice. Verse. Dialogues. The prophetic register. |
| Foreword | First person memoir | First person | "I remember my sister Suzanne typing early drafts." The only unmediated autobiographical voice. |

**Key finding:** The foreword is the only section written in Roger's direct first-person autobiographical voice. The biography proper is written *about* him in third person. The poems are written *by* him but in the prophetic/literary register, not the autobiographical.

**The Incomplete Theodolite needs the first-person voice.** The foreword — 245 words about the family, the ration from the brewery, the Miners of South Wales — is the register the Theodolite requires. Everything else is either third-person biography or formal poetry.

## 3. Voice Register: "The Legislators' Assembly"

Each biography chapter includes a section called "The Legislators' Assembly" — a dialogue between historical/literary figures and "The Subject" (Roger). This is a dramatic device:

- **Blake, Morris, Shelley, Coleridge, Pound, Graves, Thomas, Pushkin** — all speak in character
- **"The Subject"** — speaks as Roger but in a formal register
- These dialogues are the closest the biography comes to Roger's voice, filtered through the dramatic convention

Example from Book I:
> The Subject: "Yet we lost something in the transition from coal to credit. The miners could see their product, touch it, know its worth. How do we apply their wisdom to a world where wealth is numbers on screens?"

## 6. The Biographical Gap — What the Theodolite Needs

The Poetic Legislator covers:
- Grandfather (Foster Lewis, 1900s) — **well documented**
- Intellectual awakening (2000s–2010s) — **well documented**
- Activism/publishing (2011–2024) — **well documented via Appendix B blog index**

The Poetic Legislator does NOT cover:
- Childhood and upbringing — **absent**
- Education (surveying qualification) — **absent**
- Early career (property development) — **absent**
- Family life — **245 words in Foreword only**
- The specific moment of transition from developer to critic — **absent**
- How a chartered surveyor came to build a FAIR-Index — **the gap**

**This is the "missing leg" the Incomplete Theodolite names.**

The theodolite has three readings: the miner (documented), the developer-as-critic (documented), the key worker (documented). But the *person between the miner and the critic* — the grandson who became a surveyor, who built houses, who discovered the scales were wrong — exists in the Poetic Legislator only as "the biographical subject" in third person, with the formative decades elided.

The Incomplete Theodolite Chapter 1 partially bridges this with N03 ("He did not set out to. He set out to build houses. The scales were wrong.") — but the full biographical material for that sentence does not exist in any published text. It is the autobiography that has not been written yet.

## Summary

The Biography of a Poetic Legislator is **not an autobiography.** It is a third-person critical biography with a companion poetry anthology. The only first-person autobiographical passage is the 245-word foreword.

The biographical spine runs from 1902 (Foster Lewis) to 2024 (AI synthesis), with a **40-year gap** (~1965–2005) covering the personal life the author has chosen not to narrate. The poems and essays are first-person prophetic voice, not autobiographical.

The Incomplete Theodolite's Chapter 1 correctly identifies this gap as "the missing leg." The check-weighman's grandson became a surveyor became a critic — but the *becoming* is the unwritten chapter. The Poetic Legislator documents the before (the miner) and the after (the critic). The middle is the dough that has not yet been forged.
