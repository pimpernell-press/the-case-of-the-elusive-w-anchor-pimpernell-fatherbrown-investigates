# Chapter 7: The Wanking in Circles Problem

*In which Father Brown maps the complaints process, Flambeau loses
his temper, and the word "wanking" is used in its proper Anglo-Saxon
context for the first time in a theological discussion.*

---

It was Flambeau who said it. Father Brown would not have used the
word himself — not from prudishness, but because he felt the English
language contained sufficient terms of ecclesiastical disapproval
without borrowing from the anatomical. But Flambeau, having spent
forty-five minutes reading Roger's complaint correspondence, slammed
the folder shut and said:

"They're just wanking in circles."

Father Brown considered this.

"That is," he said after a moment, "both vulgar and *precisely*
accurate. I shall adopt it as a technical term."

---

## The Circle

The correspondence ran as follows, and Father Brown laid it out on
the vestry table like a man reconstructing a crime scene from
bloodstains:

**Day 1:** Roger emails Heart Internet. "My domain has been
redirected. Please fix it."

**Day 3:** Heart Internet replies. "This is a registry-level issue.
Please contact Nominet."

**Day 4:** Roger emails Nominet. "My domain has been redirected.
Heart Internet says it's your issue."

**Day 8:** Nominet replies. "We don't manage individual domain
settings. Please contact your registrar."

**Day 9:** Roger emails Heart Internet. "Nominet says it's your
issue."

**Day 14:** Heart Internet replies. "We've looked into it and
can't see a problem. The domain appears to be resolving correctly."
(It was, by now, resolving correctly — because of the fix applied
on March 2nd, before Roger's complaint. See Chapter 5.)

**Day 15:** Roger replies. "It *was* broken. I have screenshots."

**Day 21:** Heart Internet replies. "We can only investigate
current issues. If the domain is working now, we consider this
matter resolved."

**Day 22:** Roger emails Nominet. "Heart Internet won't
investigate because the problem has been fixed. But no one will
tell me *why* it happened."

**Day 30:** Nominet replies. "We recommend contacting your
registrar for historical DNS queries."

---

"Do you see it?" said Father Brown.

"I see a man being bounced between two organisations like a tennis
ball," said Flambeau, "except both players are on the same side of
the net."

"Precisely. And the ball — Roger — is expected to provide the
energy for every bounce. Each reply costs him time. Each redirect
costs him effort. Each 'please contact the other department' is a
small tax on his patience, his working hours, and his faith in
institutional competence. The organisations lose nothing. They
spend thirty seconds composing a redirect email. Roger spends
thirty minutes composing a detailed response. The economics are
*designed* to exhaust the complainant."

Father Brown drew a circle on a napkin.

"This is not a failure of the complaints process," he said. "This
*is* the complaints process. The circle is not a bug. The circle
is the product. It is engineered to produce exactly one outcome:
the complainant gives up. And when the complainant gives up, the
file is closed, the metrics improve, and everyone congratulates
themselves on their resolution rate."

"What's the resolution rate?" asked Flambeau.

"One hundred percent," said Father Brown. "Every complaint is
resolved. Most of them are resolved by the complainant dying of
old age, but resolved nonetheless."
